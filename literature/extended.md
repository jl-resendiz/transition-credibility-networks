# Verified reference library for climate transition risk network paper

**Of the 19 papers submitted for verification, 18 are confirmed correct and one contains a critical journal/volume/page error.** The Puller (2007) citation incorrectly attributes the paper to the *American Economic Review* when it was published in the *Review of Economics and Statistics*—the AER citation actually belongs to Fabrizio, Rose, and Wolfram (2007). All other citations check out. Below is the full verification with 13 additional papers identified to fill thematic gaps, organized by priority.

## GAP 3: Informational cascades and cascading revelation

### Paper 3A — Bikhchandani, Hirshleifer & Welch (1992) ✅ VERIFIED

**Citation:** Bikhchandani, Sushil, David Hirshleifer, and Ivo Welch (1992). "A Theory of Fads, Fashion, Custom, and Cultural Change as Informational Cascades." *Journal of Political Economy*, 100(5), 992–1026.
**DOI:** 10.1086/261849
**Status:** Published.
**Core finding:** An informational cascade occurs when it becomes optimal for individuals to ignore their own private information and follow predecessors' actions. Cascades explain localized behavioral conformity but are inherently fragile—small public-information shocks can shatter them, causing abrupt shifts in mass behavior.
**Relevance:** Foundational theory for how transition-risk signals cascade through networks of power utilities: once a critical mass of utilities acts on decarbonization signals, others follow regardless of private information, but fragility means cascades can also collapse.

### Paper 3B — Banerjee (1992) ✅ VERIFIED

**Citation:** Banerjee, Abhijit V. (1992). "A Simple Model of Herd Behavior." *Quarterly Journal of Economics*, 107(3), 797–817.
**DOI:** 10.2307/2118364
**Status:** Published.
**Core finding:** In a sequential decision model, rational agents optimally mimic predecessors, generating herd behavior in equilibrium. The resulting herding is socially inefficient because society loses the informational value of individuals' private signals.
**Relevance:** Complements Bikhchandani et al. by providing the alternative canonical herding framework; relevant to explaining why utilities in a network may cluster around similar transition strategies despite diverse private assessments of transition risk.

### Paper 3C — Farmer et al. (2019) ✅ VERIFIED

**Citation:** Farmer, J. Doyne, Cameron Hepburn, Matthew C. Ives, Thomas Hale, Thom Wetzer, Penny Mealy, Ryan Rafaty, Sugandha Srivastav, and Rupert Way (2019). "Sensitive intervention points in the post-carbon transition." *Science*, 364(6436), 132–134.
**DOI:** 10.1126/science.aaw7287
**Status:** Published.
**Note:** Published title uses lowercase ("post-carbon transition"), not title case.
**Core finding:** Analogous to physical tipping points, "sensitive intervention points" (SIPs) in socioeconomic and technological systems can be exploited to trigger self-amplifying feedback effects that deliver outsized decarbonization impact. The authors identify two SIP types: those triggering cascades and those shifting equilibria.
**Relevance:** Directly motivates the cascade mechanism in the paper—small policy interventions at SIPs in the power sector can propagate nonlinearly through the utility network.

### Paper 3D — Scheffer et al. (2009) ✅ VERIFIED

**Citation:** Scheffer, Marten, Jordi Bascompte, William A. Brock, Victor Brovkin, Stephen R. Carpenter, Vasilis Dakos, Hermann Held, Egbert H. van Nes, Max Rietkerk, and George Sugihara (2009). "Early-warning signals for critical transitions." *Nature*, 461(7260), 53–59.
**DOI:** 10.1038/nature08227
**Status:** Published.
**Core finding:** Complex systems—ecosystems, financial markets, climate—exhibit generic early-warning signals (increased autocorrelation, rising variance) as they approach critical tipping points. These indicators emerge from "critical slowing down" near bifurcation points.
**Relevance:** Provides the theoretical basis for detecting whether the global power-utility network is approaching a critical transition in which decarbonization cascades become self-sustaining.

### Paper 3E — Way et al. (2022) ✅ VERIFIED

**Citation:** Way, Rupert, Matthew C. Ives, Penny Mealy, and J. Doyne Farmer (2022). "Empirically grounded technology forecasts and the energy transition." *Joule*, 6(9), 2057–2082.
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.joule.2022.08.009
**Status:** Published (Open Access, CC BY 4.0).
**Core finding:** Using probabilistic cost-forecasting methods validated on 50+ technologies, the authors show a rapid green energy transition will likely produce net savings of many trillions of dollars relative to continued fossil fuel dependence. Energy-economy models used by the IPCC have systematically overestimated renewable costs and underestimated deployment.
**Relevance:** Provides the empirical foundation for positive-feedback cost dynamics in renewables—falling costs accelerate adoption, which further reduces costs—central to the cascade mechanism among utilities.

### Paper 3F — Acemoglu et al. (2011) ✅ VERIFIED

**Citation:** Acemoglu, Daron, Munther A. Dahleh, Ilan Lobel, and Asuman Ozdaglar (2011). "Bayesian Learning in Social Networks." *Review of Economic Studies*, 78(4), 1201–1236.
**DOI:** 10.1093/restud/rdr004
**Status:** Published.
**Core finding:** In a sequential learning model over a general social network, asymptotic learning (convergence to the correct action) occurs with unbounded private beliefs as long as there is sufficient "expansion in observations." With bounded beliefs, cascades can prevent correct learning depending on network topology.
**Relevance:** Provides the formal network-learning theory: whether utilities converge on correct transition strategies depends on network structure and signal informativeness—directly applicable to spatial network topology among global power utilities.

### Additional 3G — Smith & Sørensen (2000): Sequential learning with increasing signal informativeness

**Citation:** Smith, Lones and Peter Sørensen (2000). "Pathological Outcomes of Observational Learning." *Econometrica*, 68(2), 371–398.
**DOI:** 10.1111/1468-0262.00113
**Status:** Published.
**Core finding:** Generalizes the Bikhchandani-Hirshleifer-Welch framework by distinguishing bounded from unbounded private beliefs. With **unbounded beliefs** (where later-arriving agents can receive arbitrarily strong signals), cascades never persist permanently—sufficiently informative signals break incorrect herds and guarantee asymptotic learning.
**Relevance:** Directly addresses the "opposite of standard Bayesian convergence" request: as climate science, technology costs, and regulatory frameworks crystallize, later-moving utilities receive increasingly precise signals that can break incorrect herds (e.g., continued coal investment), enabling correct learning cascades.

### Additional 3H — Otto et al. (2020): Social tipping dynamics in the energy sector

**Citation:** Otto, Ilona M., Jonathan F. Donges, Roger Cremades, Avit Bhowmik, Richard J. Hewitt, Wolfgang Lucht, Johan Rockström, Franziska Allerberger, Mark McCaffrey, Sylvanus S. P. Doe, Alex Lenferna, Nerea Morán, Detlef P. van Vuuren, and Hans Joachim Schellnhuber (2020). "Social tipping dynamics for stabilizing Earth's climate by 2050." *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*, 117(5), 2354–2365.
**DOI:** 10.1073/pnas.1900577117
**Status:** Published.
**Core finding:** Identifies six social tipping elements whose activation triggers cascade-like diffusion of technologies, behaviors, and social norms for decarbonization. In the energy sector, the key positive-feedback loop runs: adoption lowers costs → lower costs accelerate adoption, analogous to epidemiological contagion spreading through social networks.
**Relevance:** Provides the direct theoretical bridge between social tipping dynamics and energy-sector technology diffusion cascades—exactly the mechanism through which transition risk propagates spatially among power utilities.

### Additional 3I — Mercure et al. (2018): Stranded fossil fuel asset cascades

**Citation:** Mercure, J.-F., H. Pollitt, J. E. Viñuales, N. R. Edwards, P. B. Holden, U. Chewpreecha, P. Salas, I. Sognnaes, A. Lam, and F. Knobloch (2018). "Macroeconomic impact of stranded fossil fuel assets." *Nature Climate Change*, 8(7), 588–593.
**DOI:** 10.1038/s41558-018-0182-1
**Status:** Published.
**Core finding:** Stranded fossil fuel assets will materialize partly from an already-ongoing technological trajectory, irrespective of new climate policies. The resulting discounted global wealth loss of **US$1–4 trillion**—comparable to the 2008 financial crisis—constitutes a "carbon bubble" with cascading macroeconomic consequences.
**Relevance:** Demonstrates that stranded-asset risks propagate through interconnected economic systems in a domino-like cascade, directly analogous to how one utility's transition decision affects neighboring utilities' economic calculus through the spatial network.

## GAP 1: Fama-MacBeth inference and HAC standard errors

### Paper 1A — Fama & MacBeth (1973) ✅ VERIFIED

**Citation:** Fama, Eugene F. and James D. MacBeth (1973). "Risk, Return, and Equilibrium: Empirical Tests." *Journal of Political Economy*, 81(3), 607–636.
**DOI:** 10.1086/260061
**Status:** Published.
**Core finding:** Introduces the two-step cross-sectional regression methodology: estimate betas from time-series regressions, then run period-by-period cross-sectional regressions of returns on betas, averaging the slope coefficients over time. The authors find results consistent with the CAPM.
**Relevance:** The Fama-MacBeth procedure is the foundational method for testing whether network-based exposure to climate transition risk is priced cross-sectionally among power utilities.

### Paper 1B — Newey & West (1987) ✅ VERIFIED

**Citation:** Newey, Whitney K. and Kenneth D. West (1987). "A Simple, Positive Semi-Definite, Heteroskedasticity and Autocorrelation Consistent Covariance Matrix." *Econometrica*, 55(3), 703–708.
**DOI:** 10.2307/1913610
**Status:** Published (Notes and Comments section).
**Core finding:** Proposes a HAC covariance matrix estimator guaranteed to be positive semi-definite by construction, using a Bartlett kernel with declining weights on sample autocovariances at increasing lags.
**Relevance:** Essential for correcting Fama-MacBeth time-series standard errors for serial correlation in the slope coefficients, given that climate transition risk propagation likely exhibits persistence.

### Paper 1C — Petersen (2009) ✅ VERIFIED

**Citation:** Petersen, Mitchell A. (2009). "Estimating Standard Errors in Finance Panel Data Sets: Comparing Approaches." *Review of Financial Studies*, 22(1), 435–480.
**DOI:** 10.1093/rfs/hhn053
**Status:** Published.
**Core finding:** Systematically compares standard error methods in finance panels, showing that clustering by firm addresses firm-level correlation, Fama-MacBeth addresses cross-sectional correlation (time effects), and **two-way clustering** is needed when both dependence types are present.
**Relevance:** Key methodological guide for choosing between Fama-MacBeth with HAC, clustered SEs, or two-way clustering when utility-level residuals are correlated both across firms (network dependence) and time (persistent policy regimes).

### Additional 1D — Cameron, Gelbach & Miller (2011) ✅ FOUND

**Citation:** Cameron, A. Colin, Jonah B. Gelbach, and Douglas L. Miller (2011). "Robust Inference With Multiway Clustering." *Journal of Business & Economic Statistics*, 29(2), 238–249.
**DOI:** 10.1198/jbes.2010.07136
**Status:** Published.
**Core finding:** Proposes a variance estimator for OLS and nonlinear estimators that enables cluster-robust inference under two-way or multiway non-nested clustering. The method combines cluster-robust estimates along each dimension and subtracts the intersection, and is easily implemented in standard software.
**Relevance:** Enables simultaneous clustering by firm and time period (or country and time) when studying transition risk transmission in panels where residuals are correlated along multiple dimensions—a natural robustness check against Fama-MacBeth with HAC.

### Additional 1E — Conley, Gonçalves & Hansen (2018): Spatial/network inference

**Citation:** Conley, Timothy G., Sílvia Gonçalves, and Christian B. Hansen (2018). "Inference with Dependent Data in Accounting and Finance Applications." *Journal of Accounting Research*, 56(4), 1139–1203.
**DOI:** 10.1111/1475-679X.12219
**Status:** Published.
**Core finding:** Unifies HAC estimators, Driscoll-Kraay, multiway clustering, and Fama-MacBeth within a common theoretical framework. Simulations show that **sample-splitting procedures (including Fama-MacBeth) are most likely to deliver robust inference** in large heterogeneous panels, especially when two-way clustering imposes overly restrictive dependence structure.
**Relevance:** Directly addresses when Fama-MacBeth with HAC outperforms two-way clustering—particularly when cross-sectional dependence is complex (as with network-based transition risk that doesn't align neatly with firm or country clusters). Also discusses spatial HAC methods potentially adaptable to network distance.

## GAP 4: "Already priced" interpretation for carbon price

### Paper 4A — Merton (1987) ✅ VERIFIED

**Citation:** Merton, Robert C. (1987). "A Simple Model of Capital Market Equilibrium with Incomplete Information." *Journal of Finance*, 42(3), 483–510.
**DOI:** 10.1111/j.1540-6261.1987.tb04565.x
**Status:** Published (AFA Presidential Address).
**Core finding:** In equilibrium with incomplete information, investors only hold securities they know about (the "investor recognition hypothesis"). Securities with lower recognition require **higher expected returns** to compensate for undiversified risk borne by a smaller investor base.
**Relevance:** Provides the theoretical basis for why transition risk may be priced differently across jurisdictions: utilities in mature carbon markets enjoy higher investor recognition of carbon risk, potentially fully pricing it in, while utilities in newer jurisdictions face an "information shadow" where transition risk is not yet recognized.

### Paper 4B — Calel & Dechezleprêtre (2016) ✅ VERIFIED

**Citation:** Calel, Raphael and Antoine Dechezleprêtre (2016). "Environmental Policy and Directed Technological Change: Evidence from the European Carbon Market." *Review of Economics and Statistics*, 98(1), 173–191.
**DOI:** 10.1162/REST_a_00470
**Status:** Published.
**Note:** Author name correctly includes accent: Dechezleprêtre.
**Core finding:** The EU ETS increased low-carbon innovation among regulated firms by approximately **10%** without crowding out other patenting, but induced only a modest ~1% increase in total European low-carbon patenting—no spillover to non-regulated firms.
**Relevance:** Documents the EU ETS as a functioning carbon pricing mechanism that has shaped firm behavior for nearly two decades, supporting the argument that EU-regulated utilities have had ample time to incorporate carbon costs into valuations.

### Additional 4C — McLean & Pontiff (2016): Diminishing information content of known signals

**Citation:** McLean, R. David and Jeffrey Pontiff (2016). "Does Academic Research Destroy Stock Return Predictability?" *Journal of Finance*, 71(1), 5–32.
**DOI:** 10.1111/jofi.12365
**Status:** Published.
**Core finding:** Across 97 cross-sectional return predictors, portfolio returns are 26% lower out-of-sample and **58% lower post-publication**, with the 32% incremental decline attributable to investors learning about the anomaly. This provides direct evidence that repeated exposure to a pricing signal diminishes its marginal information value for generating abnormal returns.
**Relevance:** Empirical analog for the "already priced" interpretation: the carbon price signal has been observed in the EU for nearly two decades, and its capacity to generate abnormal returns among utility stocks has likely diminished substantially.

### Additional 4D — Montagnoli & de Vries (2010): EU ETS market efficiency maturation

**Citation:** Montagnoli, Alberto and Frans P. de Vries (2010). "Carbon Trading Thickness and Market Efficiency." *Energy Economics*, 32(6), 1331–1336.
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.eneco.2010.04.001
**Status:** Published.
**Core finding:** Variance ratio tests show the EU ETS was informationally inefficient during the Phase I trial period but **restored market efficiency in Phase II**, documenting the carbon market's progressive maturation toward informational efficiency.
**Relevance:** Directly demonstrates that the EU carbon market has matured from inefficient to efficient, implying that carbon-cost exposure of utilities in mature EU ETS jurisdictions is increasingly reflected in equity prices—supporting the "already priced" hypothesis.

### Additional 4E — Choi, Gao & Jiang (2020): Diminished climate-news reactions in mature jurisdictions

**Citation:** Choi, Darwin, Zhenyu Gao, and Wenxi Jiang (2020). "Attention to Global Warming." *Review of Financial Studies*, 33(3), 1112–1145.
**DOI:** 10.1093/rfs/hhz086
**Status:** Published (RFS Climate Finance special issue).
**Core finding:** Stock market attention to climate change increases during abnormally warm weather, causing carbon-intensive stocks to underperform. Crucially, the authors document that **countries with earlier climate engagement show weaker market reactions**, providing direct evidence that stock market sensitivity to climate signals attenuates in mature jurisdictions.
**Relevance:** The strongest empirical support for the "already priced" interpretation: utilities in EU countries with long-standing carbon pricing show diminished market reactions to climate news, while utilities in newly exposed jurisdictions react more strongly—exactly the cross-jurisdictional heterogeneity the paper would predict.

## GAP 2: Market power and Cournot competition in electricity markets

### Paper 2A — Bushnell, Mansur & Saravia (2008) ✅ VERIFIED

**Citation:** Bushnell, James B., Erin T. Mansur, and Celeste Saravia (2008). "Vertical Arrangements, Market Structure, and Competition: An Analysis of Restructured US Electricity Markets." *American Economic Review*, 98(1), 237–266.
**DOI:** 10.1257/aer.98.1.237
**Status:** Published.
**Core finding:** Simulates Cournot equilibrium prices in three restructured US electricity markets and finds that vertical arrangements (long-term contracts, vertical integration) **dramatically reduce** the exercise of market power. Without vertical arrangements (as in California), production inefficiencies would exceed 45%.
**Relevance:** Demonstrates that market structure fundamentally shapes pricing and therefore the pass-through of carbon costs, directly affecting how transition risk transmits across the utility network.

### Paper 2B — Puller (2007) ⚠️ CRITICAL ERROR CORRECTED

**User-provided citation (INCORRECT):** Puller, Steven L. (2007). "Pricing and Firm Conduct in California's Deregulated Electricity Market." *American Economic Review*, 97(4), 1250–1277.

**Corrected citation:** Puller, Steven L. (2007). "Pricing and Firm Conduct in California's Deregulated Electricity Market." ***Review of Economics and Statistics***, **89**(1), **75–87**.
**DOI:** 10.1162/rest.89.1.75
**Status:** Published.
**Errors found:** The journal is *REStat* not *AER*; the volume is 89 not 97; the issue is 1 not 4; the pages are 75–87 not 1250–1277. The AER 97(4) 1250–1277 citation actually corresponds to Fabrizio, Rose, and Wolfram (2007), "Do Markets Reduce Costs?"
**Core finding:** Using firm-level California electricity market data (1998–2000), finds that firm conduct was **fairly consistent with Cournot pricing** for most of the sample. The dramatic 2000 price spike was driven more by cost/demand changes than by changes in strategic conduct, though generators priced slightly above Cournot levels.
**Relevance:** Empirically validates Cournot as a reasonable model for electricity markets. Under Cournot, firms with market power can pass through more than 100% of carbon cost increases, creating windfall profits—a key mechanism for transition risk amplification.

### Paper 2C — Borenstein, Bushnell & Wolak (2002) ✅ VERIFIED

**Citation:** Borenstein, Severin, James B. Bushnell, and Frank A. Wolak (2002). "Measuring Market Inefficiencies in California's Restructured Wholesale Electricity Market." *American Economic Review*, 92(5), 1376–1405.
**DOI:** 10.1257/000282802762024557
**Status:** Published.
**Core finding:** Decomposes wholesale electricity expenditures into production costs, competitive rents, and market-power rents. During summer 2000, **59% of the $6.94 billion expenditure increase** was attributable to market power exercise.
**Relevance:** Provides a decomposition methodology directly applicable to separating cost-driven from market-power-driven carbon cost pass-through, central to quantifying how transition risk propagates through electricity networks.

### Paper 2D — Wolfram (1999) ✅ VERIFIED

**Citation:** Wolfram, Catherine D. (1999). "Measuring Duopoly Power in the British Electricity Spot Market." *American Economic Review*, 89(4), 805–826.
**DOI:** 10.1257/aer.89.4.805
**Status:** Published.
**Core finding:** Prices exceeded marginal costs in the British duopoly electricity market, but markups were **substantially lower than Cournot or joint-monopoly predictions**, suggesting regulatory constraints, entry threats, and forward contracts restrained market power.
**Relevance:** Shows that actual market power may be moderated by regulatory factors, implying that carbon cost pass-through models must account for regulatory and contractual environments across different jurisdictions in the utility network.

### Additional 2E — Lundin & Tangerås (2020): Explicit Cournot validation

**Citation:** Lundin, Erik and Thomas P. Tangerås (2020). "Cournot Competition in Wholesale Electricity Markets: The Nordic Power Exchange, Nord Pool." *International Journal of Industrial Organization*, 68, 102536.
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.ijindorg.2019.102536
**Status:** Published.
**Core finding:** Demonstrates that horizontal bid-curve shifts in wholesale electricity markets are consistent with Cournot competition. Applied to Nord Pool (2011–2013), the authors **reject perfect competition** and estimate an average price-cost margin of approximately 4%.
**Relevance:** The most explicit empirical test finding Cournot as the best-fitting competition model in a major electricity market, providing direct support for modeling carbon cost pass-through under Cournot assumptions.

### Additional 2F — Borenstein, Bushnell & Knittel (1999): HHI limitations in electricity

**Citation:** Borenstein, Severin, James Bushnell, and Christopher R. Knittel (1999). "Market Power in Electricity Markets: Beyond Concentration Measures." *The Energy Journal*, 20(4), 65–88.
**DOI:** 10.5547/ISSN0195-6574-EJ-Vol20-No4-3
**Status:** Published.
**Core finding:** Standard HHI measures are inadequate for electricity markets because they ignore demand elasticity, capacity constraints, and cost heterogeneity. Market power becomes severe at high-demand hours even when HHI values appear moderate.
**Relevance:** Demonstrates that simple concentration measures understate market power in electricity, implying that transition-risk transmission models must use simulation-based or structural approaches rather than relying on HHI alone.

## GAP 6: Grieser et al. network estimation methodology

### Paper 6A — Grieser, Hadlock, LeSage & Zekhnini (2022) ✅ FOUND AND VERIFIED

**Citation:** Grieser, William, Charles J. Hadlock, James P. LeSage, and Morad Zekhnini (2022). "Network Effects in Corporate Financial Policies." *Journal of Financial Economics*, 144(1), 247–272.
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.jfineco.2021.05.060
**Status:** Published.
**Core finding:** Applies spatial autoregressive (SAR) models with product-market network weight matrices to estimate peer effects in corporate capital structure. Detects **significant peer-effect leverage coefficients around 0.20**, indicating strategic complementarity, but argues that prior estimates substantially overstated the magnitude. More central firms exert larger aggregate effects on network peers.
**Relevance:** Directly foundational—demonstrates how to use SAR models with network-based weight matrices to estimate spillover effects in corporate finance, the exact methodology for studying climate transition risk propagation through utility networks.

### Paper 6B — LeSage & Pace (2009) ✅ VERIFIED

**Citation:** LeSage, James P. and R. Kelley Pace (2009). *Introduction to Spatial Econometrics*. Boca Raton, FL: Chapman & Hall/CRC Press (Taylor & Francis). 374 pp.
**ISBN:** 978-1-4200-6424-7 (Hardback)
**DOI:** 10.1201/9781420064254
**Status:** Published.
**Core description:** The definitive textbook on spatial econometric methods covering ML and Bayesian estimation of SAR, SEM, and spatial Durbin models. Provides the non-linear partial derivative interpretation of spillover effects (direct vs. indirect) and model comparison techniques.
**Relevance:** Core methodological reference for all spatial econometric models used to estimate network transmission of transition risk.

### Additional 6C — Lam & Souza (2020): Weight matrix estimation and selection

**Citation:** Lam, Clifford and Pedro C. L. Souza (2020). "Estimation and Selection of Spatial Weight Matrix in a Spatial Lag Model." *Journal of Business & Economic Statistics*, 38(3), 693–710.
**DOI:** 10.1080/07350015.2019.1569526
**Status:** Published.
**Core finding:** Proposes a unified framework for estimating the best linear combination of multiple candidate spatial weight matrices using the **adaptive LASSO** for selection. The approach allows data-driven determination of which connectivity structures matter, avoiding misspecification risk when multiple weight matrices are plausible.
**Relevance:** Provides the methodology for selecting among geographic proximity, grid connectivity, trade linkages, and ownership ties as network structures for the utility spatial weight matrix.

### Additional 6D — Ozdagli & Weber (2025): SAR models in financial networks

**Citation:** Ozdagli, Ali K. and Michael Weber (2025). "Monetary Policy through Production Networks: Evidence from the Stock Market." *Review of Financial Studies*, forthcoming (advance article).
**DOI:** 10.1093/rfs/hhaf106
**Status:** Published (RFS advance article, 2025).
**Core finding:** Uses spatial autoregressions derived from input-output production networks to decompose monetary policy effects on stock prices into direct effects and higher-order network effects. **55–85% of the overall stock market impact** is attributed to network effects.
**Relevance:** Serves as a methodological template for using SAR models with economic network weight matrices to decompose direct vs. network spillover effects in asset prices—directly analogous to decomposing transition risk into direct and network-transmitted components among utilities.

### Additional 6E — Debarsy & LeSage (2022): Bayesian model averaging for multiple weight matrices

**Citation:** Debarsy, Nicolas and James P. LeSage (2022). "Bayesian Model Averaging for Spatial Autoregressive Models Based on Convex Combinations of Different Types of Connectivity Matrices." *Journal of Business & Economic Statistics*, 40(2), 547–558.
**DOI:** 10.1080/07350015.2020.1840993
**Status:** Published.
**Core finding:** Develops Bayesian model averaging for SAR models using **convex combinations** of multiple connectivity matrices. Posterior model probabilities assess the relative importance of each connectivity type while accounting for model uncertainty.
**Relevance:** Provides the Bayesian methodology for formally assessing which types of utility-network connectivity (geographic, grid, ownership, trade) matter most for transition risk transmission, while properly handling model uncertainty.

## GAP 5: Cross-sectional return dispersion

### Paper 5A — Stivers (2003) ✅ VERIFIED

**Citation:** Stivers, Christopher T. (2003). "Firm-level return dispersion and the future volatility of aggregate stock market returns." *Journal of Financial Markets*, 6(3), 389–411.
**DOI:** 10.1016/S1386-4181(02)00044-7
**Status:** Published.
**Core finding:** Cross-sectional return dispersion has **significant predictive power** for future aggregate stock market volatility. The channel posits that dispersion captures heterogeneity in firm-level responses to macroeconomic shocks, signaling future aggregate uncertainty.
**Relevance:** Directly supports using return dispersion among power utilities as an indicator of heterogeneous information processing—elevated dispersion around transition-risk events signals differential utility-level exposure and aggregate sectoral uncertainty.

### Paper 5B — Connolly & Stivers (2003) ✅ FOUND AND VERIFIED

**Citation:** Connolly, Robert and Chris Stivers (2003). "Momentum and Reversals in Equity-Index Returns During Periods of Abnormal Turnover and Return Dispersion." *Journal of Finance*, 58(4), 1521–1556.
**DOI:** 10.1111/1540-6261.00576
**Status:** Published.
**Note:** Published in *Journal of Finance*, not JFQA or JFM.
**Core finding:** Equity-index return autocorrelation increases with unexpected cross-sectional dispersion. Weeks with extreme turnover and dispersion shocks coincide with more macroeconomic news, suggesting **dispersion signals heterogeneous information processing** among investors.
**Relevance:** Directly relevant—when major climate policy events occur, heterogeneous processing across utilities with different transition exposures generates elevated dispersion, which this paper shows has predictable return-dynamic consequences.

### Additional 5C — Diether, Malloy & Scherbina (2002): Dispersion as disagreement

**Citation:** Diether, Karl B., Christopher J. Malloy, and Anna Scherbina (2002). "Differences of Opinion and the Cross Section of Stock Returns." *Journal of Finance*, 57(5), 2113–2141.
**DOI:** 10.1111/0022-1082.00490
**Status:** Published.
**Core finding:** Stocks with higher analyst forecast dispersion earn **lower future returns**, consistent with Miller's (1977) overvaluation hypothesis under short-sale constraints. Establishes cross-sectional dispersion as a valid proxy for disagreement about fundamentals.
**Relevance:** Foundational justification for interpreting return dispersion among utilities as reflecting disagreement about how transition policies affect different firms—utilities about which investors disagree most regarding transition risk are those with highest dispersion.

### Additional 5D — Loungani, Rush & Tave (1990): Seminal return dispersion paper

**Citation:** Loungani, Prakash, Mark Rush, and William Tave (1990). "Stock market dispersion and unemployment." *Journal of Monetary Economics*, 25(3), 367–388.
**DOI:** 10.1016/0304-3932(90)90059-D
**Status:** Published.
**Core finding:** Cross-sectional stock return dispersion across industries **significantly predicts unemployment**, capturing the intensity of sectoral reallocation. Higher dispersion signals greater need for labor reallocation across sectors.
**Relevance:** The seminal paper establishing that return dispersion captures structural economic change. Directly analogous: as some utilities shift to renewables while others remain fossil-dependent, elevated dispersion signals the magnitude of transition-driven sectoral restructuring.

### Additional 5E — Stivers & Sun (2010): Dispersion as state variable

**Citation:** Stivers, Chris and Licheng Sun (2010). "Cross-Sectional Return Dispersion and Time Variation in Value and Momentum Premiums." *Journal of Financial and Quantitative Analysis*, 45(4), 987–1014.
**DOI:** 10.1017/S0022109010000384
**Status:** Published.
**Core finding:** Return dispersion positively predicts the value premium and negatively predicts the momentum premium, functioning as a **leading countercyclical state variable** with incremental information content beyond market returns.
**Relevance:** Establishes return dispersion as a legitimate ICAPM state variable, supporting the interpretation that elevated dispersion during transition events reflects shifts in the investment opportunity set for power utilities.

### Additional 5F — Chichernea, Holder & Petkevich (2015): Dispersion and economic transitions

**Citation:** Chichernea, Doina C., Anthony D. Holder, and Alex Petkevich (2015). "Does return dispersion explain the accrual and investment anomalies?" *Journal of Accounting and Economics*, 60(1), 133–148.
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.jacceco.2015.03.001
**Status:** Published.
**Core finding:** Return dispersion captures **"uncertainty associated with economic transitions and the flexibility of adaptability to fundamental economic restructuring."** Firms with higher RD exposure earn different risk premiums, explaining accrual and investment anomalies.
**Relevance:** The most direct conceptual bridge between return dispersion and economic transitions. Explicitly interprets dispersion as reflecting the uncertainty of structural transformation—precisely the mechanism in the climate/energy transition context.
