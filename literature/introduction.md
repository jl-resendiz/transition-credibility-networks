# Framing references for a paper on spatial climate transition risk

All target papers below have been verified for existence, citation details, and core arguments. This review identifies **10–12 canonical references** across three framing blocks, organized by rhetorical function in the introduction. Each entry includes the exact citation, core finding, and a concrete suggestion for how to deploy it in your opening paragraphs.

---

## Block 1: Climate finance as a macro-asset pricing problem

This block establishes the stakes: pricing climate transition risk is among the most important unsolved problems in financial economics, with trillions of dollars of asset value exposed.

---

### 1A. The definitive surveys of climate finance

**Giglio, Stefano, Bryan Kelly, and Johannes Stroebel (2021). "Climate Finance." *Annual Review of Financial Economics*, 13(1), 15–36.**

**Core finding:** The most comprehensive survey of the climate finance field. Reviews approaches to incorporating climate risk in macro-finance models, empirical evidence on climate risk pricing across real estate, equities, and fixed income, and portfolio hedging strategies. Identifies major open questions including how to measure firm-level transition exposure and how climate risks propagate across asset classes. (~800–1,000 citations.)

**How to use it:** Open the introduction by citing this survey to establish that climate finance has emerged as a major subfield: *"Pricing climate transition risk has become one of the defining challenges in financial economics (Giglio, Kelly, and Stroebel, 2021), yet the field lacks tools to capture how transition shocks propagate spatially across interconnected firms."* This positions your paper as addressing a gap the survey itself identifies.

---

**Stroebel, Johannes and Jeffrey Wurgler (2021). "What Do You Think about Climate Finance?" *Journal of Financial Economics*, 142(2), 487–498.**

**Core finding:** Survey of 861 finance academics, professionals, and regulators. By an overwhelming margin, respondents believe **asset prices underestimate climate risks**. Regulatory/transition risk is ranked as the top climate risk over a 5-year horizon, while physical risks dominate over 30 years. Carbon taxes and institutional investors are viewed as the most powerful forces for change. (~500–700 citations.)

**How to use it:** Cite alongside Giglio et al. to strengthen the claim that transition risk is both financially material and systematically mispriced: *"A broad consensus among finance academics and practitioners holds that markets underestimate climate transition risk (Stroebel and Wurgler, 2021), motivating the search for better measurement frameworks."*

---

**Hong, Harrison, G. Andrew Karolyi, and José A. Scheinkman (2020). "Climate Finance." *The Review of Financial Studies*, 33(3), 1011–1023.**

**Core finding:** Editorial framing piece for the landmark RFS special issue on climate finance. Defines the field as "the study of local and global financing of public and private investment that seeks to support mitigation of and adaptation to climate change." Outlines the research program spanning market efficiency, beliefs heterogeneity, divestment, stranded assets, and financial stability. (~400–600 citations.)

**How to use it:** Optional supplementary cite alongside Giglio et al. (2021) if you want to note that the field's major journals have dedicated special issues to these questions. Can be used parenthetically: *"(see Hong, Karolyi, and Scheinkman, 2020, for the scope of this research program)."*

---

### 1B. Macro-finance models of climate uncertainty and asset prices

**Barnett, Michael, William Brock, and Lars Peter Hansen (2020). "Pricing Uncertainty Induced by Climate Change." *The Review of Financial Studies*, 33(3), 1024–1066.**

**Core finding:** Develops a framework integrating robust control and ambiguity aversion with asset pricing to value climate uncertainty. Identifies three compounding uncertainty channels — carbon dynamics, temperature dynamics, and economic damage functions — and shows that **ambiguity aversion about model misspecification substantially increases the social cost of carbon**. Demonstrates that Knightian uncertainty, not just measurable risk, is central to pricing climate change. (~350–500 citations.)

**How to use it:** Cite to argue that even at the macro level, pricing climate risk is notoriously difficult due to deep uncertainty: *"The difficulty is compounded by the fact that climate transition risk involves not merely measurable risk but deep Knightian uncertainty across multiple interacting systems (Barnett, Brock, and Hansen, 2020). Our paper introduces a complementary dimension: spatial network structure as a determinant of how this uncertainty resolves at the firm level."*

---

**Nordhaus, William (2019). "Climate Change: The Ultimate Challenge for Economics." *American Economic Review*, 109(6), 1991–2014.**

**Core finding:** Nobel Prize lecture presenting the integrated assessment framework (DICE model) linking economic growth, emissions, climate change, and damages. Argues climate change is **a global negative externality that resists control by both markets and national governments**, making it the ultimate coordination problem. Advocates carbon pricing as the most efficient policy instrument. (~1,500–2,000+ citations.)

**How to use it:** Cite for gravitas in the opening to frame climate transition as a first-order economic challenge: *"Climate change represents the ultimate challenge for economics (Nordhaus, 2019), and its financial dimension — how transition risk reprices assets — has become a central concern for investors and regulators alike."*

---

**Daniel, Kent D., Robert B. Litterman, and Gernot Wagner (2019). "Declining CO₂ Price Paths." *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*, 116(42), 20886–20891.** (Circulated as NBER WP 22795, 2016, under the title "Applying Asset Pricing Theory to Calibrate the Price of Climate Risk.")

**Core finding:** Applies Epstein-Zin asset pricing preferences to climate policy. Shows that the optimal carbon price is **high today and declining over time** — the opposite of standard IAM predictions — because the insurance value of mitigation falls as uncertainty resolves. Higher risk aversion increases both the carbon price and the associated risk premium. (~250–400 citations.)

**How to use it:** Optional cite to reinforce that asset pricing theory has direct implications for climate policy and transition timing: *"Asset pricing frameworks applied to climate change imply that the financial cost of delayed transition is front-loaded (Daniel, Litterman, and Wagner, 2019), underscoring the urgency of accurate risk measurement."*

---

### 1C. The magnitude of stranded assets and global financial exposure

**McGlade, Christophe and Paul Ekins (2015). "The Geographical Distribution of Fossil Fuels Unused When Limiting Global Warming to 2°C." *Nature*, 517(7533), 187–190.**

**Core finding:** The landmark "unburnable carbon" paper. Shows that meeting the 2°C target requires leaving **one-third of oil reserves, half of gas reserves, and over 80% of coal reserves** in the ground through 2050. Maps the geographical distribution of these stranded reserves. (~3,000–4,500+ citations.)

**How to use it:** Cite to establish the physical scale of transition risk and its inherently spatial character: *"The transition to a low-carbon economy implies that a vast share of fossil fuel reserves must remain unextracted (McGlade and Ekins, 2015), concentrating stranded-asset risk in specific geographies — yet current financial models rarely capture how this spatial concentration propagates through economic networks."* This directly motivates your paper's spatial approach.

---

**Dietz, Simon, Alex Bowen, Charlie Dixon, and Philip Gradwell (2016). "'Climate Value at Risk' of Global Financial Assets." *Nature Climate Change*, 6(7), 676–679.**

**Core finding:** Estimates the climate value at risk (VaR) of global financial assets. Expected climate VaR is **1.8% of global assets under business-as-usual**, but the 99th-percentile tail risk reaches **16.9% (~$24.2 trillion)**. Limiting warming to 2°C substantially truncates this tail. Reframes climate change as a mainstream financial risk management problem. (~700–1,000 citations.)

**How to use it:** Cite to quantify the financial stakes: *"With up to $24 trillion in global financial assets at risk under tail climate scenarios (Dietz et al., 2016), understanding precisely where and how transition risk materializes is no longer optional."*

---

**van der Ploeg, Frederick and Armon Rezai (2020). "Stranded Assets in the Transition to a Carbon-Free Economy." *Annual Review of Resource Economics*, 12(1), 281–298.**

**Core finding:** Reviews how fossil fuel assets face stranding from uncertain policy tightening and renewable technology breakthroughs. Stranding risk concentrates in the **20 largest oil, gas, and coal companies** (responsible for over one-third of historical warming) but extends to carbon-intensive industries. A disorderly transition amplifies financial instability through stranded capital stock. (~200–350 citations.)

**How to use it:** Cite to connect stranded assets to systemic risk: *"The concentration of stranded-asset risk in a small number of highly connected firms and sectors (van der Ploeg and Rezai, 2020) suggests that transition risk may be far more systemic than firm-level analyses imply — a hypothesis our spatial network framework directly tests."*

## Block 2: The failure of "isolated" ESG scores

This block argues that the current paradigm — treating transition credibility as a firm-level attribute via ESG ratings or net-zero pledges — is deeply flawed, motivating an alternative network-based approach.

---

### 2A. ESG ratings diverge and fail to capture fundamental risk

**Berg, Florian, Julian F. Kölbel, and Roberto Rigobon (2022). "Aggregate Confusion: The Divergence of ESG Ratings." *Review of Finance*, 26(6), 1315–1344.**

**Core finding:** The canonical paper on ESG rating divergence. Compares ratings from six major providers and finds average pairwise correlations of only **38–71%**. Decomposes divergence into measurement (56%), scope (38%), and weight (6%). Detects a "rater effect" whereby a provider's overall view of a firm biases its measurement of specific categories. Concludes that ESG ratings are **fundamentally noisy signals**, not merely differently weighted composites. (~2,500–3,000+ citations.)

**How to use it:** This is the central cite for your critique of firm-level ESG approaches: *"The financial industry's dominant approach to transition risk — ESG ratings — suffers from well-documented 'aggregate confusion,' with major providers disagreeing on even basic assessments of the same firm (Berg, Kölbel, and Rigobon, 2022). Our paper sidesteps this noise entirely by measuring transition exposure through hard physical and economic network linkages rather than subjective ratings."*

---

**Chatterji, Aaron K., Rodolphe Durand, David I. Levine, and Samuel Touboul (2016). "Do Ratings of Firms Converge? Implications for Managers, Investors and Strategy Researchers." *Strategic Management Journal*, 37(8), 1597–1614.**

**Core finding:** Documents a "surprising lack of agreement" across six CSR rating providers, with low convergent validity persisting even after adjusting for definitional differences. Establishes that the divergence problem predates and is more fundamental than measurement methodology — the ratings have **low validity** as measures of actual corporate social responsibility. (~700–900 citations.)

**How to use it:** Optional supplementary cite to show the ESG divergence problem is long-standing and structural: *"This divergence is not a recent data problem but a deep structural feature of subjective firm-level assessment (Chatterji et al., 2016; Berg et al., 2022)."*

---

### 2B. Voluntary disclosure is insufficient for systemic risk assessment

**Christensen, Hans Bonde, Luzi Hail, and Christian Leuz (2021). "Mandatory CSR and Sustainability Reporting: Economic Analysis and Literature Review." *Review of Accounting Studies*, 26(3), 1176–1248.**

**Core finding:** Comprehensive economic analysis of mandatory sustainability disclosure. Shows that **firm-level incentives create substantial variation in reporting outcomes even under common standards** — convergence requires alignment of underlying incentives, which rarely occurs. Voluntary disclosure regimes are particularly unreliable because firms strategically select what and how to report. (~500–700 citations.)

**How to use it:** Cite to argue that better disclosure alone cannot solve the measurement problem: *"Even mandatory sustainability reporting fails to produce consistent, comparable data across firms (Christensen, Hail, and Leuz, 2021), let alone capture the systemic, network-mediated dimensions of transition risk that our framework addresses."*

---

### 2C. Investors know climate risk is material but lack measurement tools

**Krueger, Philipp, Zacharias Sautner, and Laura T. Starks (2020). "The Importance of Climate Risks for Institutional Investors." *The Review of Financial Studies*, 33(3), 1067–1111.**

**Core finding:** Survey of 439 institutional investors reveals they **believe climate risks are financially material** and that equity valuations do not fully reflect them (those seeing "not enough" pricing outnumber "too much" by 20:1). Long-horizon, larger investors favor engagement over divestment. Critically, investors report a gap between perceived materiality and **available tools and data** for measuring climate exposure. (~1,800–2,200 citations.)

**How to use it:** This is the key "demand-side" cite establishing that the problem your paper solves is real and urgent: *"Institutional investors overwhelmingly believe climate risks are financially material yet report lacking adequate tools to measure portfolio-level exposure (Krueger, Sautner, and Starks, 2020). Our spatial network approach offers precisely such a tool — one grounded in observable economic linkages rather than subjective firm-level scores."*

---

### 2D. Supplementary references for Block 2

**Bénabou, Roland and Jean Tirole (2010). "Individual and Corporate Social Responsibility." *Economica*, 77(305), 1–19.** The canonical theory paper explaining why CSR signaling is inherently noisy — firms can mimic green behavior without genuine commitment due to mixed motives of altruism, image-seeking, and material incentives. (~2,000–2,500 citations.) Use to provide theoretical grounding for why firm-level self-reporting is unreliable.

**NGFS (2019). *A Call for Action: Climate Change as a Source of Financial Risk.* First Comprehensive Report, April 2019.** The foundational central banking report establishing that climate risks are a source of financial risk within central banks' mandates, and calling for bridging critical data gaps. Use to note that even regulators acknowledge the measurement infrastructure is inadequate.

## Block 3: The call for "network" and "systemic" climate risk

This block makes the positive case that transition risk is a network property — shocks propagate through financial and physical supply chains — and positions your paper's spatial network framework as the answer to this recognized need.

---

### 3A. Climate transition risk propagates through networks

**Battiston, Stefano, Antoine Mandel, Irene Monasterolo, Franziska Schütze, and Gabriele Visentin (2017). "A Climate Stress-Test of the Financial System." *Nature Climate Change*, 7(4), 283–288.**

**Core finding:** The landmark paper applying network-based stress testing to climate policy shocks. Finds that **direct and indirect exposures to climate-policy-relevant sectors represent 45–48% of Euro Area investors' equity portfolios**. Demonstrates that climate transition shocks propagate through the interbank and cross-holding network, with second-round (indirect) effects amplifying first-round losses. Concludes that early, credible policy allows smooth adjustment while delays risk systemic disruption. (~1,200–1,500+ citations.)

**How to use it:** This is the single most important cite for Block 3 — it establishes the precedent for network-based climate stress testing: *"Battiston et al. (2017) demonstrated that climate policy shocks propagate through financial networks, with indirect exposures amplifying direct losses. Our paper extends this logic from financial cross-holdings to spatial economic networks, showing that geographic supply-chain linkages create an additional, previously unmeasured channel of transition risk transmission."*

---

**Battiston, Stefano, Irene Monasterolo, Keywan Riahi, and Bas J. van Ruijven (2021). "Accounting for Finance Is Key for Climate Mitigation Pathways." *Science*, 372(6545), 918–920.**

**Core finding:** Argues that Integrated Assessment Models used for climate scenarios **fail to model the financial system**, creating a critical blind spot. The financial system can either enable or hamper the transition: if investors delay revising expectations and then shift suddenly, this triggers financial instability and a costlier, disorderly transition. Proposes a framework capturing the interdependence between investor expectations and real investment allocation. (~250–400 citations.)

**How to use it:** Cite to argue for the necessity of integrating financial network structure into climate models: *"As Battiston et al. (2021) argue, omitting the financial system from climate transition models creates a dangerous blind spot. Our framework fills a related gap by incorporating the spatial economic network through which transition shocks actually propagate."*

---

### 3B. Network topology determines whether shocks amplify or dissipate

**Acemoglu, Daron, Asuman Ozdaglar, and Alireza Tahbaz-Salehi (2015). "Systemic Risk and Stability in Financial Networks." *American Economic Review*, 105(2), 564–608.**

**Core finding:** Establishes the theoretical foundation for how network structure determines systemic fragility. Shows a **phase transition**: when shocks are small, denser interconnections enhance stability through diversification; beyond a critical threshold, the same interconnections become channels for shock amplification and cascading failure. Network topology — not just individual firm health — determines system-wide outcomes. (~1,000+ citations.)

**How to use it:** This is the key theoretical bridge from general network economics to your specific application: *"Network theory has established that systemic risk exhibits phase transitions — the same interconnections that diversify small shocks amplify large ones (Acemoglu, Ozdaglar, and Tahbaz-Salehi, 2015). Climate transition, with its potential for large, correlated policy shocks, may push spatial economic networks past precisely such thresholds."*

---

**Bolton, Patrick, Morgan Després, Luiz Awazu Pereira da Silva, Frédéric Samama, and Romain Svartzman (2020). *The Green Swan: Central Banking and Financial Stability in the Age of Climate Change.* Bank for International Settlements (BIS).**

**Core finding:** Introduces the concept of **"green swan" risks** — potentially extremely disruptive climate-related financial events characterized by radical uncertainty, nonlinearity, and cascade effects. Argues that traditional backward-looking risk models are fundamentally inadequate for climate risks and calls for forward-looking, scenario-based, system-wide analysis by central banks. (~1,500+ citations.)

**How to use it:** Cite to connect your paper to the highest-level policy discourse: *"Central banks have warned that climate transition may trigger 'green swan' events — systemic crises propagating through cascade effects that conventional risk models cannot capture (Bolton et al., 2020). Our spatial network framework provides the micro-level architecture through which such cascades would unfold."*

---

### 3C. Systems-level thinking in high-impact science journals

**Farmer, J. Doyne, Cameron Hepburn, Matthew C. Ives, Thomas Hale, Thom Wetzer, Penny Mealy, Ryan Rafaty, Sugandha Srivastav, and Rupert Way (2019). "Sensitive Intervention Points in the Post-Carbon Transition." *Science*, 364(6436), 132–134.**

**Core finding:** Proposes the concept of **sensitive intervention points (SIPs)** — socioeconomic analogues to climate tipping points. Identifies two types: "kicks" (shifting the system's current state) and "shifts" (altering underlying dynamics). Argues that climate transition requires **systems-level thinking** because nonlinear feedbacks in technology, policy, and finance can amplify small interventions into large systemic shifts. (~400–600 citations.)

*Note: The correct author list includes 9 authors (Farmer, Hepburn, Ives, Hale, Wetzer, Mealy, Rafaty, Srivastav, Way), not Teytelboym, who co-authored a different 2015 Farmer paper.*

**How to use it:** Cite to ground your spatial framework in the broader systems-science perspective: *"The post-carbon transition is a complex systems problem where interventions at specific nodes can cascade nonlinearly through the network (Farmer et al., 2019). Our paper identifies the spatial structure of these cascades in financial terms."*

---

### 3D. Supplementary references for Block 3

**Haldane, Andrew G. and Robert M. May (2011). "Systemic Risk in Banking Ecosystems." *Nature*, 469, 351–355.** Foundational cross-disciplinary paper applying ecological network analysis to banking. Shows that homogeneity and tight coupling amplify contagion. (~1,500+ citations.) Use as the canonical cite for network approaches to financial stability.

**Campiglio, Emanuele, Yannis Dafermos, Pierre Monnin, Josh Ryan-Collins, Guido Schotten, and Misa Tanaka (2018). "Climate Change Challenges for Central Banks and Financial Regulators." *Nature Climate Change*, 8(6), 462–468.** Perspective piece arguing the first challenge for regulators is developing comprehensive analytical frameworks for assessing climate impacts on financial stability. (~600–900 citations.) Use to reinforce that regulators are actively seeking the type of framework you provide.

**ECB/ESRB (2022). *The Macroprudential Challenge of Climate Change.* Joint ECB/ESRB Report, July 2022.** Finds that climate shocks rapidly spread through the European financial system via economic and financial linkages, with correlated exposures and overlapping portfolios creating systemic vulnerabilities. Use as a policy-authority validation that network-mediated climate risk is a real supervisory concern.

## Recommended citation map for the introduction

The following maps approximately how these references might flow across a 4–5 paragraph introduction:

**Paragraph 1 (The stakes):** Open with Nordhaus (2019) and Giglio, Kelly, and Stroebel (2021) to establish climate transition risk as a first-order challenge. Cite Stroebel and Wurgler (2021) on the consensus that markets underestimate it. Cite McGlade and Ekins (2015) and Dietz et al. (2016) to quantify the scale — trillions in stranded assets and climate VaR.

**Paragraph 2 (The current approach fails):** Pivot to Berg, Kölbel, and Rigobon (2022) as the anchor cite showing ESG ratings are fundamentally noisy. Reinforce with Chatterji et al. (2016) and Christensen, Hail, and Leuz (2021) on the structural limits of firm-level disclosure. Cite Krueger, Sautner, and Starks (2020) to show that investors themselves recognize the tools are inadequate.

**Paragraph 3 (The network alternative):** Introduce the network perspective. Cite Acemoglu, Ozdaglar, and Tahbaz-Salehi (2015) on the general theory, then Battiston et al. (2017) as the proof of concept for network-based climate stress testing. Cite Bolton et al. (2020, BIS) and Battiston et al. (2021) to argue that the policy establishment recognizes the need for system-level approaches.

**Paragraph 4 (Our contribution):** Position your spatial network framework as filling the gap between the macro-asset pricing literature (Block 1), which documents the scale of the problem, and the network finance literature (Block 3), which provides the theoretical architecture — while overcoming the noise that plagues the ESG-based approach (Block 2). Cite Farmer et al. (2019) to frame the spatial dimension as identifying where sensitive intervention points lie in the real economic network. Close by noting that your paper provides the granular, observable, network-based measure that Krueger et al.'s (2020) surveyed investors say they need.
