\begin{quotation}\small\singlespacing\noindent
\textbf{Abstract.} Does geographic distance insulate power utilities from transition risk? I study how coal plant retirements propagate to the stock prices of 703 listed utilities across 80 countries. The channel is technology, not geography: peers with similar generation portfolios lose 3.3 percentage points of market value over four months, while geographic proximity has no robust effect. The mechanism is asymmetric aggregation. Local competitive effects diversify across markets; technology obsolescence does not. Emissions trading amplifies the signal. ESG scores and fuel-mix similarity both predict transition returns but measure different things: disclosure versus physical assets.
\end{quotation}

\medskip\noindent\textit{JEL Classification:} G12, G14, Q54, L94.\\
\noindent\textit{Keywords:} transition risk, stranded assets, fuel-mix similarity, technology networks, carbon pricing.

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

Coal plants are retiring in some jurisdictions and expanding in others. Over 80 percent of coal reserves must remain unextracted if Paris targets bind \citep{McGlade2015}, placing trillions of dollars in generating assets at risk \citep{Dietz2016}. Does geographic distance insulate the expanding jurisdictions from the financial consequences of retirement elsewhere?

The evidence in this paper suggests not. When a power utility retires coal capacity, the stock prices of technologically similar peers decline, regardless of where those peers are located. A firm at the median of the fuel-similarity distribution loses 3.3 percentage points of market value over four months relative to a firm with no fuel overlap. Geographic proximity, by contrast, has no robust effect on stock returns at the firm level. The channel through which retirement shocks propagate is technology, not geography.

The mechanism is what I call asymmetric aggregation. A listed utility typically operates plants in multiple local electricity markets. When a nearby competitor exits, the utility gains market share in that local market. But these local gains are independent across markets and diversify away in the firm's aggregate stock price, much as idiosyncratic sectoral shocks wash out in a balanced production network \citep{AcemogluCarvalhoOzdaglarTahbazSalehi2012}. Technology exposure operates differently. If coal becomes uneconomic in one jurisdiction, every plant using coal faces the same obsolescence risk, regardless of where it is located. Fuel-mix similarity is a common factor. It does not diversify. This asymmetry explains why the technology channel dominates in the data: it is the only channel that survives aggregation from the plant level to the stock level.

I construct fuel-mix similarity as the $L_1$ similarity of capacity-share vectors from the Global Energy Monitor, following \citet{HobergPhillips2016}'s approach to measuring firm-pair proximity. The specification has the structure of a shift-share design \citep{GoldsmithPinkhamSorkinSwift2020}: fuel-mix shares, fixed by plant construction decisions made years before any retirement, serve as the pre-determined exposure; each retirement event serves as the shock. Four diagnostics support a causal reading: pre-determined shares using only pre-2014 plant vintages remain significant, Rotemberg weights are non-negative, and the \citet{Oster2019} coefficient stability bound indicates that unobservables would need to be 21 times more important than observables to explain away the result. The sample covers 703 listed utilities across 80 countries and 175 first-mover retirement events spanning 35 countries.

Two additional findings emerge. First, emissions trading amplifies the technology channel: the stranding penalty roughly doubles for firms in jurisdictions with an emissions trading system. Second, ESG environmental scores and fuel-mix similarity both predict transition returns, but they measure different things. ESG reflects what firms disclose about their transition plans. Fuel-mix similarity reflects the physical generation assets they actually operate. Both survive a joint test, meaning that knowing one does not make the other redundant.

These results speak to a question that arises whenever climate policy is unilateral. If only some countries retire fossil capacity, are the others insulated? The asset pricing evidence says no. The market penalises coal-heavy utilities in proportion to their fuel-mix similarity to the retiring firm, regardless of jurisdiction. Coal retirements send technology signals whose financial reach extends well beyond the country where the retirement occurs. The paper documents this pattern; it does not assess whether the retirements themselves are desirable.

The closest antecedents are the carbon premium literature \citep{Bolton2021, BoltonKacperczyk2023} and the network propagation framework \citep{AcemogluCarvalhoOzdaglarTahbazSalehi2012}. The carbon premium literature documents that carbon-intensive firms earn higher expected returns, but does not explain how the premium propagates across firms. The network propagation literature shows that idiosyncratic shocks transmit through interconnected sectors, but studies production networks, not technology networks in the power sector. I connect the two: the fuel-mix network has a common-factor structure that explains why the carbon premium exists and how it propagates. The cosine-similarity methodology follows \citet{HobergPhillips2016}. The contagion-versus-competition decomposition builds on \citet{LangStulz1992}.

\section{A Model of Networked Transition Risk}\label{sec:model}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

Power utilities operate physical plants in local electricity markets. Each plant uses a specific generation technology. When a plant retires, two consequences follow. Locally, competitors gain market share as residual demand shifts to remaining generators. Globally, every firm using similar technology faces a question: does the retirement signal that the technology itself is becoming obsolete? These two consequences aggregate to the stock level in fundamentally different ways. The local effect diversifies across markets. The technology effect does not.

\subsection{Environment}\label{sec:environment}

\paragraph{Agents and timing.}
Consider an economy with $N$ listed power utilities indexed by $i \in \{1,\ldots,N\}$ and a continuum of identical risk-averse investors. Time is discrete with three dates: $t = 0$ (ex ante), $t = 1$ (signal arrival), and $t = 2$ (terminal payoff).

\paragraph{Investor preferences.}
The representative investor has CRRA preferences over terminal wealth:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:utility}
    U = \E_0\!\left[\frac{W_2^{1-\gamma}}{1-\gamma}\right], \qquad \gamma > 1.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Firm structure.}
Firm $i$ operates plants in $K_i \geq 1$ local electricity markets. Let $\theta_{ik}$ denote the revenue share of market $k$ in firm $i$'s total operations, with $\sum_{k=1}^{K_i} \theta_{ik} = 1$. The quantity $K_i$ varies across firms: some utilities operate in a single market, others span dozens of jurisdictions.

\paragraph{Network structure.}
Firms are connected through two independently constructed, row-normalised adjacency matrices. The first measures geographic proximity:
\begin{equation}
    w^{\text{geo}}_{ij} = \frac{\exp(-d_{ij}/\bar{d})/d_{ij}}{\sum_{k\neq i}\exp(-d_{ik}/\bar{d})/d_{ik}},
\end{equation}
where $d_{ij}$ is the great-circle distance between capacity-weighted plant centroids and $\bar{d}$ is a bandwidth parameter. The second measures fuel-mix similarity: $w^{\text{fuel}}_{ij}$ is the $L_1$ similarity of capacity-share vectors, row-normalised.

The two matrices differ in a property that will prove consequential. Geographic proximity varies at the plant level: two firms may be close in one market and distant in another. Fuel-mix similarity is defined at the firm level: it depends on the entire generation portfolio, not on any single plant.

\paragraph{Regulatory environment.}
The economy operates under one of two regulatory regimes, $\omega \in \{0,1\}$, unobserved by investors at $t = 0$. Under the lax regime ($\omega = 0$), fossil generation faces no technology penalty. Under the strict regime ($\omega = 1$), fossil generation is subject to an obsolescence cost proportional to the firm's fossil intensity $\alpha_i \in [0,1]$. At $t = 0$, investors assign prior probability $p_0 = \Pr(\omega = 1)$ to the strict regime.

\paragraph{Restructuring shocks.}
At $t=1$, a subset of firms retire fossil capacity. The shock vector $\mathbf{s} \geq 0$ records the magnitude of each retirement. Investors observe $\mathbf{s}$ and update their beliefs about $\omega$ through Bayes' rule:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bayes}
    p_1(\mathbf{s}) = \frac{p_0\, L(\mathbf{s})}{p_0\, L(\mathbf{s}) + (1-p_0)},
\end{equation}
where $L(\mathbf{s}) = f_1(\mathbf{s})/f_0(\mathbf{s})$ is the likelihood ratio. Retirements are more likely under the strict regime, so $L$ is increasing in the magnitude and frequency of shocks.

\paragraph{Cash flows.}
Firm $i$'s terminal cash flow is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cashflows}
    \Pi_i = \bar{\Pi} + \sum_{k=1}^{K_i} \theta_{ik}\, G_{ik}(\mathbf{s}) - \omega \cdot F_i(\mathbf{s},\alpha_i) + \eta_i,
\end{equation}
where $\bar{\Pi} > 0$ is baseline profitability, $G_{ik}$ is the competitive benefit in local market $k$, $F_i$ is the technology penalty, and $\eta_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2_\eta)$ is idiosyncratic noise. The two middle terms capture the two consequences of a restructuring shock. Their precise form is specified below.

\subsection{Two Channels}\label{sec:channels}

The cash flow equation~(\ref{eq:cashflows}) contains two opposing effects of a retirement shock. The first operates locally, through the physical electricity grid. The second operates globally, through shared generation technology.

\begin{assumption}[Local Market Competition]\label{ass:competition}
    When a geographically proximate firm $j$ retires capacity $s_j$, the residual demand for firm $i$'s plant in market $k$ increases by $w^{\mathrm{geo},k}_{ij}\, s_j$, where $w^{\mathrm{geo},k}_{ij}$ measures proximity in market $k$ specifically.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Technological Obsolescence]\label{ass:obsolescence}
    Under the strict regulatory state ($\omega = 1$), the retirement of fuel-similar capacity imposes a penalty proportional to fuel similarity $w^{\mathrm{fuel}}_{ij}$ and shock size $s_j$.
\end{assumption}

\begin{lemma}[Local Competitive Benefit]\label{lem:geo}
    In a Cournot equilibrium with $n_i + 1$ symmetric generators in market $k$, facing linear inverse demand $P(Q) = a - bQ$ with marginal cost $c < a$, if one competitor exits, firm $i$'s profit increases by:
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:cournot_profit}
        \Delta\pi_{ik} = \frac{(a-c)^2}{b}\left[\frac{1}{n_i^2} - \frac{1}{(n_i+1)^2}\right] > 0.
    \end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
    Standard Cournot. With $m$ symmetric firms, each earns $\pi^* = (a-c)^2/[b(m+1)^2]$. Exit reduces $m$ by one. The difference is strictly positive since $n_i < n_i + 1$. \qed
\end{proof}

The geographic benefit aggregates over plants:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:G_function}
    \sum_{k=1}^{K_i} \theta_{ik}\, G_{ik}(\mathbf{s}) = \delta \sum_{k=1}^{K_i} \theta_{ik} \sum_j w^{\text{geo},k}_{ij}\, s_j, \qquad \delta > 0.
\end{equation}

The technology penalty operates under the strict regime ($\omega = 1$):
\begin{equation}\label{eq:F_function}
    F_i(\mathbf{s},\alpha_i) = \beta \alpha_i + \phi \sum_{j} w^{\text{fuel}}_{ij}\, s_j, \qquad \beta, \phi > 0.
\end{equation}

The first term is a direct cost of fossil intensity. The second is the network penalty: the market infers that firm $i$'s technology is vulnerable because a fuel-similar peer has exited.

\subsection{Asymmetric Aggregation}\label{sec:aggregation}

The geographic and technology channels enter the cash flow equation~(\ref{eq:cashflows}) symmetrically: both are weighted sums over peer shocks. But they aggregate to the stock level in asymmetric ways.

The reason is structural. Geographic proximity varies at the plant level. A firm with plants in Germany and Brazil has different geographic neighbours in each market. Fuel-mix similarity is defined at the firm level. A firm with 60 percent coal has 60 percent coal in every market it operates in.

\begin{lemma}[Aggregation Attenuation]\label{lem:aggregation}
    The stock-level geographic exposure of firm $i$ is:
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:aggregation}
        \beta^{\mathrm{stock}}_{\mathrm{geo},i} = A\delta \sum_{k=1}^{K_i} \theta_{ik} \sum_j w^{\mathrm{geo},k}_{ij} s_j.
    \end{equation}
    If local competitive shocks are independent across markets (i.e., $\mathrm{Cov}(G_{ik}, G_{ik'}) = 0$ for $k \neq k'$), the variance of geographic exposure scales as $\mathrm{Var}(\beta^{\mathrm{stock}}_{\mathrm{geo},i}) \propto \sum_k \theta_{ik}^2 \leq 1/K_i$. For a firm operating in many markets ($K_i$ large), the geographic component diversifies away.

    In contrast, the fuel-similarity penalty:
    \begin{equation}
        \beta^{\mathrm{stock}}_{\mathrm{fuel},i} = -Ap_1\phi \sum_j w^{\mathrm{fuel}}_{ij} s_j
    \end{equation}
    does not attenuate with $K_i$ because $w^{\mathrm{fuel}}_{ij}$ is a property of the firm's entire generation portfolio, not any single local market.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
    The geographic term in~(\ref{eq:cashflows}) is $\delta \sum_k \theta_{ik} \sum_j w^{\text{geo},k}_{ij} s_j$. Under independence of local markets, the variance of this sum equals $\delta^2 \sum_k \theta_{ik}^2 \, \text{Var}(\sum_j w^{\text{geo},k}_{ij} s_j)$. By the constraint $\sum_k \theta_{ik} = 1$ and Cauchy-Schwarz, $\sum_k \theta_{ik}^2 \leq 1$ with equality only when $K_i = 1$, and $\sum_k \theta_{ik}^2 \leq 1/K_i$ when revenue shares are equal. As $K_i \to \infty$, the geographic variance vanishes. The fuel term $\phi \sum_j w^{\text{fuel}}_{ij} s_j$ is defined at the firm level and does not decompose into independent market components. \qed
\end{proof}

The logic parallels the diversification argument in production networks \citep{AcemogluCarvalhoOzdaglarTahbazSalehi2012}. In a balanced network, idiosyncratic shocks wash out at rate $1/\sqrt{N}$. In a star network, the hub transmits shocks to all nodes and diversification fails. Here, the fuel-mix network has a common-factor structure: technology obsolescence is the hub, and all firms sharing that technology are the spokes. Local competitive effects, by contrast, are independent across markets and diversify away at rate $1/\sqrt{K_i}$.

\subsection{Equilibrium and Predictions}\label{sec:predictions}

\begin{definition}[Equilibrium]\label{def:equilibrium}
    An equilibrium consists of prices $\{V_{i,1}\}_{i=1}^N$ at $t = 1$ such that each price satisfies the SDF pricing equation $V_{i,1} = \E_1[m_{1,2}\, \Pi_i]$, where $m_{1,2} = \beta_d (W_2/W_1)^{-\gamma}$, and the representative investor holds the market portfolio. The constant $A \equiv \beta_d \E_1[(W_2/W_1)^{-\gamma}] > 0$ collects the SDF terms that are common across firms.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Technology Dominance]\label{prop:tech_dominance}
    Under Assumptions~\ref{ass:competition}--\ref{ass:obsolescence} and Lemma~\ref{lem:aggregation}, a restructuring shock $\mathbf{s}$ transmits to stock prices primarily through fuel-mix similarity:
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item[(i)] The fuel-similarity coefficient is $-Ap_1\phi < 0$ (systematic).
        \item[(ii)] The geographic coefficient is $A\delta\,H_i \approx 0$ for diversified firms, where $H_i = \sum_k \theta_{ik}^2$ is the Herfindahl index of market concentration (idiosyncratic).
        \item[(iii)] The difference $\beta_{\mathrm{fuel}} - \beta_{\mathrm{geo}}$ is strictly negative for any $p_1 > 0$.
    \end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
    Part (i) follows from Assumption~\ref{ass:obsolescence} and $p_1 > 0$. Part (ii): by Lemma~\ref{lem:aggregation}, the variance of geographic exposure scales as $H_i$, which vanishes for diversified firms. When local shocks have heterogeneous signs across markets, the mean geographic effect tends to zero by a law of large numbers. Part (iii): the difference is $-Ap_1\phi - A\delta H_i < 0$ since $p_1\phi > 0$ and $\delta H_i \geq 0$. \qed
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Asymmetric Aggregation]\label{cor:asymmetry}
    The cross-sectional average return around a restructuring shock is negative, driven by the technology penalty:
    \begin{equation}
        \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N \Delta V_{i,1} \approx -Ap_1\phi\,\overline{w^{\mathrm{fuel}} s} < 0.
    \end{equation}
\end{corollary}

\begin{proposition}[Policy Amplification]\label{prop:ets}
    The fuel coefficient $-Ap_1\phi$ is increasing in absolute value in the posterior belief $p_1$. Since $p_1$ depends on the prior $p_0$ through equation~(\ref{eq:bayes}), jurisdictions with higher prior probability of strict enforcement produce a more negative fuel coefficient. In particular, if $p_0^{\mathrm{high}} > p_0^{\mathrm{low}}$, then $p_1^{\mathrm{high}} > p_1^{\mathrm{low}}$ for any common shock $\mathbf{s}$, and:
    \begin{equation}
        \left|\frac{\partial V_{i,1}}{\partial (\sum_j w^{\mathrm{fuel}}_{ij}s_j)}\right|_{p_0^{\mathrm{high}}} > \left|\frac{\partial V_{i,1}}{\partial (\sum_j w^{\mathrm{fuel}}_{ij}s_j)}\right|_{p_0^{\mathrm{low}}}.
    \end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
    The fuel coefficient is $-Ap_1\phi$, where $p_1 = p_0 L / (p_0 L + 1 - p_0)$ from equation~(\ref{eq:bayes}). Since $\partial p_1 / \partial p_0 = L / (p_0 L + 1 - p_0)^2 > 0$, a higher prior produces a higher posterior for any realisation of $\mathbf{s}$. The fuel coefficient is linear in $p_1$, so it is more negative when $p_0$ is higher. \qed
\end{proof}

The model generates three testable predictions.

\begin{prediction}\label{pred:fuel}
The fuel-similarity coefficient is negative. The geographic coefficient is attenuated for firms operating across many markets.
\end{prediction}

\begin{prediction}\label{pred:dominance}
The fuel coefficient dominates the geographic coefficient in the cross-section: the technology channel is systematic, the competitive channel is idiosyncratic.
\end{prediction}

\begin{prediction}\label{pred:policy}
The fuel penalty is larger in jurisdictions where investors assign higher prior probability to strict enforcement of transition policy.
\end{prediction}

\section{Empirical Strategy}\label{sec:empirical}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Sample}\label{sec:sample}

The sample comprises global listed power utilities identified by SIC codes 4911, 4931, and 4932 in Compustat Global/NA, cross-referenced with plant ownership records in the Global Energy Monitor (GEM). The final sample contains 703 firms across 80 countries. Of these, 565 have complete spatial weight data for all three network layers. Within this set, 153 also have ESG environmental scores from LSEG Eikon.

The event set includes 175 first-mover retirement events with exact or announcement dates, spanning 35 countries. Of the 175 events, 117 produce at least 20 firms per event for Fama-MacBeth regressions. The event-firm panel contains 55,580 observations. Figure~\ref{fig:world_map} maps the retirement events and sample firms.

\subsection{Data Sources}\label{sec:data}

Financial statements come from Compustat Global/NA. Equity returns come from CRSP total returns for US firms and LSEG Eikon TR.TotalReturn (including dividends and corporate actions) for non-US firms. For non-US firms not covered by Eikon, I use deduplicated Compustat Global Security price returns, retaining one security per gvkey-date by highest trading volume \citep{IncePorter2006}. The final sample comprises 703 unique firms with monthly return coverage. Carbon pricing data come from the World Bank Carbon Pricing Dashboard. ESG environmental scores come from LSEG Eikon.

Monthly returns are capped at $\pm 100\%$ per \citet{IncePorter2006}. Eikon returns are filtered to end-of-month observations, and illiquid firms (more than half of months at the return cap) are removed.

\subsection{Weight Matrices}\label{sec:weights}

\paragraph{Geographic proximity ($\bW^{\mathrm{geo}}$).} I compute pairwise great-circle distances between capacity-weighted plant centroids using GPS coordinates from GEM:
\[
  w^{\text{geo}}_{ij} = \frac{\exp(-d_{ij}/\bar{d})/d_{ij}}{\sum_{k\neq i}\exp(-d_{ik}/\bar{d})/d_{ik}},
\]
where $\bar{d} = h/\ln 2$ and $h = 1000$\,km. Bandwidth sensitivity (Table~\ref{tab:bandwidth_sensitivity}) reports results for $h \in \{250, 500, 750, 1000, 1500\}$\,km.

\paragraph{Fuel-mix similarity ($\bW^{\mathrm{fuel}}$).} I build a fuel-mix vector for each firm from GEM plant records (coal, gas, oil, wind, solar, hydro, nuclear) and measure pairwise similarity as $1 - \tfrac{1}{2}\lVert \mathbf{v}_i - \mathbf{v}_j \rVert_1$, where $\mathbf{v}_i$ is the capacity-share vector, following the product-space proximity approach of \citet{HobergPhillips2016}. The matrix is row-normalised.

\paragraph{Regulatory co-membership ($\bW^{\mathrm{reg}}$).} Binary indicator for co-membership in an emissions trading system, row-normalised.\footnote{The regulatory weight matrix is included as an empirical control for correlated policy exposure, not as a third theoretical channel. The model derives predictions for the fuel and geographic channels only.}

The pairwise correlations among the three weight layers are below 0.16, confirming that they capture distinct proximity dimensions.

\subsection{Specification}\label{sec:specification}

For each retirement event $e$, I compute the cumulative abnormal return $\text{CAR}_{ie}$ over $[-1, +3]$ months, market-adjusted using value-weighted returns. The baseline specification is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:baseline}
    \text{CAR}_{ie} = \alpha + \gamma_{\text{geo}}\, w^{\text{geo}}_i + \gamma_{\text{fuel}}\, w^{\text{fuel}}_i + \gamma_{\text{reg}}\, w^{\text{reg}}_i + \gamma_s\, \text{SameSector}_i + \varepsilon_{ie}.
\end{equation}

I report three inference approaches: (1) pooled OLS with event-clustered standard errors; (2) Fama-MacBeth with Newey-West HAC correction; (3) two-way clustering by event and firm \citep{CameronGelbachMiller2011}. The central empirical test is whether the fuel coefficient is more negative than the geographic coefficient, evaluated through the difference $\gamma_{\text{geo}} - \gamma_{\text{fuel}} > 0$.

\subsection{Identification}\label{sec:identification}

The design exploits cross-sectional variation in pre-determined network exposure around retirement events. For each event $e$, the weight vectors $w^{\text{fuel}}_{ie}$, $w^{\text{geo}}_{ie}$, and $w^{\text{reg}}_{ie}$ are constructed from installed capacity and plant locations recorded in the Global Energy Monitor before the retirement occurs. These weights are properties of the firm's generation portfolio, not of the retirement itself. Variation in $\text{CAR}_{ie}$ across firms within the same event therefore reflects differences in network exposure to the retiring firm, holding the shock constant.

\paragraph{Pre-determination of weights.}
The fuel-mix similarity matrix is computed from the full GEM plant registry as of the event date. Because plant construction and fuel choice reflect decisions made years or decades before any single retirement, the weights are plausibly exogenous to the contemporaneous stock market reaction. The $L_1$ similarity metric follows the product-space approach of \citet{HobergPhillips2016}, substituting fuel-type capacity shares for the word frequencies they use to construct firm-pair proximity from 10-K product descriptions. Row normalisation ensures that the weights sum to one within each event, so the coefficient measures the return differential between a firm with maximal fuel overlap and a firm with zero overlap.

\paragraph{Network structure and the reflection problem.}
Regressing firm $i$'s returns on weighted averages of peer characteristics raises the identification concern formalised by \citet{Manski1993}: endogenous effects, contextual effects, and correlated effects cannot be separated in the linear-in-means model with uniform group membership. The present design avoids the classical reflection problem for two reasons. First, the regressors are pre-determined network weights, not contemporaneous peer outcomes. I do not regress firm $i$'s CAR on a weighted average of peer CARs; I regress it on the weight itself, which is a function of the firm's fuel portfolio and the identity of the retiring firm. Second, as \citet{BramoulleDjebbariFortin2009} show, network structure provides identification when interactions are non-uniform. The three weight matrices generate firm-specific, asymmetric, non-nested peer groups: firm $A$'s geographic neighbour $B$ may have a fuel-mix neighbour $C$ who is not $A$'s neighbour on either dimension. This intransitivity is exactly the structure that breaks the collinearity underlying the reflection problem. The low pairwise correlation across weight layers (below 0.16) confirms that the three dimensions capture distinct proximity concepts.

\paragraph{Retirement endogeneity.}
The retirement decision is not randomly assigned. Firms may retire plants in response to declining profitability, regulatory pressure, or shifting electricity demand. If retirements are anticipated, stock prices will have already adjusted before the event window opens, attenuating the measured $\text{CAR}_{ie}$ toward zero. This anticipation bias works against finding a significant fuel channel: any significant coefficient understates the full repricing associated with the retirement signal. The gradual information diffusion literature suggests that even anticipated events produce delayed cross-sectional effects. \citet{HongStein1999} model how information percolates slowly across bounded-rational investors, generating short-horizon underreaction; \citet{CohenFrazzini2008} document that stock prices of economically linked firms incorporate shared news with a delay of one to three months. If anything, partial anticipation shifts the timing of the response, not its cross-sectional allocation across network channels.

The more serious threat is that retirements coincide with unobserved jurisdiction-level shocks that independently depress the returns of fuel-similar firms. For example, declining electricity demand might simultaneously trigger retirements and depress coal-heavy utilities. I address this through the correlated effects argument below.

\paragraph{Correlated effects.}
Firms with similar fuel portfolios may be exposed to common shocks unrelated to the retirement itself. A gas price movement or a renewable subsidy announcement, for instance, could simultaneously affect all coal-intensive utilities and coincide with a retirement event. This is the ``correlated effects'' problem in \citet{Manski1993}'s taxonomy. Three features of the design mitigate this concern.

First, the Fama-MacBeth procedure estimates equation~(\ref{eq:baseline}) separately for each event and then averages across 117 events spanning 35 countries and 15 years. For a correlated shock to bias the average coefficient, it would need to coincide systematically with retirement events across jurisdictions and time periods. Second, the specification includes three orthogonal weight layers and a same-sector indicator, absorbing variation along the geographic, regulatory, and sectoral dimensions. The fuel coefficient therefore captures residual variation in returns associated with technology similarity after these alternative channels are removed. Third, the opposing-sign structure of the channels provides a further diagnostic. \citet{LangStulz1992} show that the same corporate event (bankruptcy) produces positive competitive effects for close rivals and negative contagion effects for technologically similar peers. A correlated shock affecting all utilities in the same direction would bias both channels in the same direction; the fact that the geographic and fuel coefficients have opposing signs (positive and negative, respectively) is inconsistent with a single confounding factor driving both.

\paragraph{Shift-share (exposure design) interpretation.}
The specification has the structure of a shift-share instrument as formalised by \citet{GoldsmithPinkhamSorkinSwift2020}. Each observation is exposed through the inner product of a pre-determined \emph{share} (fuel-mix similarity $w^{\text{fuel}}_{ie}$, fixed by plant construction decisions made years before the event) and a common \emph{shift} (the retirement shock). Under the identifying assumption that shares are exogenous to changes in returns around retirement events, the fuel coefficient identifies the causal dose-response of abnormal returns to technology exposure.

Four diagnostics support this assumption. First, I restrict the fuel-mix weights to plants commissioned before 2014, fixing exposure before the main retirement wave. The Bartik coefficient using these pre-determined shares is significant under pooled inference ($t = -5.16$, $N = 24{,}070$). The Fama-MacBeth estimate is also significant ($t = -2.32$) but is based on only 3 events that meet the minimum observation threshold with pre-period weights, so the FM result should be interpreted with caution. Second, Rotemberg weights are entirely non-negative (0 of 40 events carry negative weight), with a Herfindahl index of 0.031, indicating that no single event dominates the estimate. Third, a pre-event balance test regresses $\text{CAR}_{ie}$ in the $[-5, -2]$ month window on the Bartik instrument. The coefficient is negative ($t = -1.87$, $p = 0.062$), significant at 10 percent but not at 5 percent. This suggests some pre-event comovement between exposed firms' returns and the instrument, though the magnitude is small relative to the event-window effect: the event-window Bartik $t$-statistic ($-5.16$) exceeds the pre-event $t$-statistic by a factor of 2.8. Fourth, I compute the \citet{Oster2019} coefficient stability bound, which asks how large selection on unobservables would need to be, relative to selection on observables, to drive the coefficient to zero. The bound yields $\delta^* = 20.8$: unobservables would need to be roughly 21 times more important than observables to explain away the result. For the standard $w^{\text{fuel}}$ specification, $\delta^* = 61.6$.

\paragraph{Event window.}
The $[-1, +3]$ month window is longer than the daily or weekly windows standard in announcement-driven event studies \citep{CampbellLoMacKinlay1997}. Two considerations motivate the choice. First, the retirement events in this sample are not single-day announcements with precise timestamps. Many involve regulatory approvals, corporate disclosures, and physical decommissioning spread over weeks or months. \citet{KochGrosjeanFussEdenhofer2016} use a similar multi-week window for regulatory events affecting the EU ETS, finding that political announcements produce price responses over days to weeks rather than minutes. A short window would miss the gradual incorporation of the signal. Second, the technology repricing mechanism in the model operates through belief updating about the regulatory state $\omega$ (Remark~\ref{rem:posterior}), which may unfold slowly as investors process the implications for peer firms. \citet{MenzlyOzbas2010} document that cross-industry return predictability from economic linkages operates at the monthly horizon, consistent with the timeline of technology signal absorption. The cost of a longer window is greater exposure to confounding events. The bandwidth sensitivity analysis (Table~\ref{tab:bandwidth_sensitivity}) and the Conley spatial standard errors provide partial reassurance, but the possibility of contamination from intervening events cannot be fully eliminated.

\paragraph{Scope of claims.}
The causal claim applies to the cross-sectional allocation of returns across firms with different pre-determined technology exposure. It does not extend to the aggregate effect of retirements on the utility sector as a whole, nor to any individual retirement-peer pair. The comparative result across channels is more robust than the level of any single coefficient: the same design, applied identically to three network layers, consistently finds that the fuel channel dominates. The opposing-sign pattern across geographic and fuel channels, confirmed by four independent tests (Table~\ref{tab:difference_battery}), is the paper's central finding.

\paragraph{Economic magnitude.} The fuel-similarity weight $w^{\text{fuel}}_i$ is row-normalised, so its cross-sectional standard deviation within a given event is approximately 0.004. A one-standard-deviation increase in fuel similarity is associated with a $-5.474 \times 0.004 = -2.2$ basis point decrease in the four-month CAR under event-clustered inference. This appears small in absolute terms but reflects the granularity of the row-normalised weight. A more intuitive comparison: a firm at the median of the fuel-similarity distribution ($w^{\text{fuel}} \approx 0.006$) experiences a predicted CAR of $-5.474 \times 0.006 = -3.3$ percentage points, while a firm with zero fuel similarity to the retiring firm experiences no penalty.

The 3.3 percentage point spread over four months is economically material. Three benchmarks contextualise this magnitude. First, it is comparable in order of magnitude to the annual carbon premium of roughly 2 to 4 percentage points documented by \citet{BoltonKacperczyk2023}. Second, it exceeds the monthly return predictability of 13 to 22 basis points from cross-industry economic linkages documented by \citet{MenzlyOzbas2010}, consistent with a network signal that is stronger but less frequent. Third, it falls within the range of intra-industry spillovers from corporate distress: \citet{LangStulz1992} document contagion effects of $-1.0$ percent and competitive effects of $+2.2$ percent within days of bankruptcy announcements. The fuel channel operates over months rather than days, reflecting the greater complexity of technology signals relative to discrete bankruptcy events \citep{CohenFrazzini2008}.

The difference is positive and significant: fuel-similar peers experience more negative returns than geographic neighbours. Four independent tests confirm the difference (Table~\ref{tab:difference_battery}).

\paragraph{Explanatory power.} The pooled $R^2$ of 0.007 reflects the well-known property that cross-sectional regressions of individual-event CARs are dominated by idiosyncratic noise \citep{CampbellLoMacKinlay1997}. The within-event $R^2$ under Fama-MacBeth averages 0.052, indicating that the four regressors explain roughly 5 percent of the cross-sectional return variance within a typical event. When CARs are collapsed to the firm level by averaging across events, the $R^2$ rises to 0.026, a 3.6-fold increase that confirms the model captures systematic variation obscured by event-level noise.

\subsection{The Role of Geographic Proximity}\label{sec:geo_role}

The geographic proximity channel is not robust. The sign and significance of $\gamma_{\text{geo}}$ vary across specifications: insignificant and negative in the baseline ($t = -0.41$ pooled; $t = -1.30$ Fama-MacBeth), marginally positive under alternative bandwidths ($t = 1.68$ to $1.80$ Fama-MacBeth), and insignificant under event-specific distance measures ($t = 1.32$ pooled; $t = 0.69$ Fama-MacBeth). Restricting the sample to single-country utilities, where geographic variation is most plausibly local, the coefficient remains insignificant ($t = 0.58$ pooled; $t = -1.04$ Fama-MacBeth).

This instability is consistent with the aggregation result in Lemma~\ref{lem:aggregation}. Local competitive benefits documented at the plant level by \citet{DavisHausman2016} are diversified away in the portfolios of listed utilities operating across multiple markets. The Herfindahl index of market concentration $H_i = \sum_k \theta_{ik}^2$ determines the rate of attenuation: for a firm operating in $K$ equal markets, the geographic exposure variance scales as $1/K$. That the sign is unstable, rather than stably zero, suggests the competitive and contagion components of geographic proximity \citep{LangStulz1992} partially offset, with neither dominating in the cross-section.

The fuel channel, by contrast, does not attenuate with diversification. Technology obsolescence is a property of the firm's entire generation portfolio, not of any single local market. Geographic distance does not insulate; technology similarity transmits. This asymmetry between the two channels is the core prediction of the model and the central empirical finding.

\subsection{The Fuel Signal Strengthens Over Time}\label{sec:learning}

If the market gradually learns to price technology obsolescence, the fuel coefficient should strengthen over calendar time. A continuous interaction $w^{\text{fuel}} \times \text{year}$ (centered at the median event year, 2013) is significant ($t = -2.34$, $p = 0.019$), indicating that later events carry a more negative fuel beta. Splitting events into year terciles sharpens the pattern: the earliest third (2011--2013) produces an insignificant average fuel beta of $+1.72$ ($t = 0.75$), while the middle (2013--2014) and latest (2014--2022) terciles yield $-3.58$ ($t = -3.02$) and $-5.52$ ($t = -3.58$), respectively. The fuel signal is absent in the early period and strengthens monotonically thereafter (Figure~\ref{fig:calendar_time}).

A geographic decomposition sharpens the pattern. The fuel coefficient is approximately zero in the United States ($t = 0.11$, 91 events) and strongly negative outside the United States ($-5.34$, $t = -4.10$, 84 events). The US accounts for 135 of the 344 first-mover retirements in the sample. If the market learns from accumulated signals, US retirements carry little new information because the technology obsolescence of coal has been extensively priced in prior events. Non-US retirements, which are more recent and occur in jurisdictions where the transition is less advanced, deliver a stronger signal.

A within-jurisdiction test yields weaker evidence. The interaction $w^{\text{fuel}} \times \log(1 + \text{order})$ is negative but not significant ($t = -1.57$, $p = 0.117$). The split-sample comparison (early order $\leq 2$ versus later events) produces a significant difference (mean fuel beta $-5.24$ versus $-1.52$; Welch $t = -2.48$, $p = 0.013$), but this comparison mixes within-jurisdiction and across-jurisdiction variation.

The calendar-time strengthening is consistent with \citet{HongStein1999}'s model of gradual information diffusion, in which bounded-rational investors slowly incorporate the implications of technology signals. In the early period, few retirements had occurred and the technology obsolescence interpretation was not yet salient. As retirements accumulated across jurisdictions, the market learned to associate fuel-mix similarity with stranding risk. The US saturation result suggests that this learning process is now largely complete in the most active retirement market but continues elsewhere.


\subsection{Robustness}\label{sec:robustness}

\paragraph{Bandwidth sensitivity.}
At each of the five bandwidth specifications ($h = 250, 500, 750, 1000, 1500$\,km), the fuel channel is significant at the 5 percent level, with Fama-MacBeth $t$-statistics ranging from $-2.28$ to $-2.65$. At each bandwidth, the geographic channel is marginally significant at the 10 percent level ($t$ from $1.68$ to $1.80$) but does not reach 5 percent at any bandwidth.

\paragraph{Conley spatial standard errors.}
At 1000\,km, the fuel channel retains $t = -4.16$, confirming that the result is not driven by residual spatial correlation.

\paragraph{Romano-Wolf multiple testing.}
Across 9 hypotheses (3 channels $\times$ 3 event windows), zero survive the Romano-Wolf stepdown correction at the 5 percent level. Four of nine survive raw significance. This result requires careful interpretation. The correction penalises the fuel channel at the primary $[-1, +3]$ window (raw $t = -7.50$) for the inclusion of six additional tests at the $[-1, +6]$ and $[-1, +12]$ windows that were not the focus of the empirical design. The joint F-test, which pools information across all three channels at the primary window, is highly significant ($F = 70.83$, permutation $p = 0.000$), confirming that the three-channel model has joint explanatory power. Restricting attention to the primary window alone, the fuel channel's $t$-statistic of $-7.50$ exceeds the Bonferroni-corrected threshold for three tests ($0.05/3 = 0.017$, critical $|t| = 2.39$) by a factor of three. The multiple testing concern is therefore a consequence of testing across redundant horizons, not of weak evidence at the primary horizon.

\paragraph{Wild cluster bootstrap.}
For the phase-out specification with 14 country clusters, the wild bootstrap $p$-value is 0.091, confirming marginal significance despite the small number of clusters.

\paragraph{Shift-share (Bartik) specification.}
The shift-share diagnostics reported in Section~\ref{sec:identification} confirm that the fuel coefficient survives when exposure is frozen at pre-2014 plant vintages ($t = -5.16$), Rotemberg weights are non-negative, and the Oster bound yields $\delta^* = 20.8$.

Additional robustness tables (weight correlations, placebo shuffle, control sensitivity, specification progression, and bandwidth sensitivity) are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:robustness}.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Technology Exposure as Systematic Risk}\label{sec:systematic}

The fuel-mix similarity channel transmits to stock prices because technology obsolescence is a common factor. If coal becomes uneconomic in one jurisdiction, all firms with high coal shares face the same physics regardless of location. In the language of asset pricing, fuel similarity measures exposure to a non-diversifiable technology factor. This connects the carbon premium literature \citep{Bolton2021, BoltonKacperczyk2023} with the network propagation framework of \citet{AcemogluCarvalhoOzdaglarTahbazSalehi2012}: the fuel-mix network has a common-factor structure in which a shock to one node (coal technology) propagates to all connected nodes simultaneously.

The opposing signs of the two channels replicate, in a climate setting, the contagion-versus-competition pattern identified by \citet{LangStulz1992} and \citet{JorionZhang2007} in corporate distress. Fuel similarity transmits contagion: shared technology exposure means that one firm's obsolescence signals vulnerability for all similar firms. Geographic proximity would transmit competitive benefit: a rival's exit raises residual demand for remaining local generators \citep{DavisHausman2016}. The model predicts that the contagion channel dominates at the stock level because it operates as a common factor, while the competitive channel diversifies away across markets (Lemma~\ref{lem:aggregation}).

The strengthening of the fuel signal over calendar time (Section~\ref{sec:learning}) is consistent with the gradual-information-diffusion framework of \citet{HongStein1999}. In their model, information about fundamental value percolates slowly across bounded-rational investors, producing short-horizon underreaction that corrects as more participants parse the signal. \citet{CohenFrazzini2008} and \citet{MenzlyOzbas2010} document analogous patterns in customer-supplier and cross-industry settings, where economic linkages generate return predictability at the monthly horizon that decays with analyst coverage and institutional ownership. The fuel-mix network operates through the same mechanism: technology linkages are harder to observe than sector membership, so the market requires successive signals before fully pricing the exposure.

\subsection{Policy Implications}\label{sec:policy}

The results confirm that geographic distance does not insulate utilities from transition risk; technology similarity is the transmission channel. Under pooled inference, emissions trading amplifies the fuel channel, suggesting that credible enforcement may be necessary for technology signals to reprice fully. The extensive margin (having an ETS) matters more than the intensive margin (the carbon price level), consistent with \citet{ChoiGaoJiang2020} who document that countries with earlier climate engagement show weaker marginal reactions to subsequent climate signals. However, the ETS interaction is not fuel-specific: a placebo test shows that geographic proximity also responds to ETS membership. The amplification may therefore reflect a broader repricing of network exposures in ETS jurisdictions rather than a technology-specific mechanism. This finding, combined with the Fama-MacBeth sign reversal, means the ETS result should be treated as suggestive.

For practitioners, the implication is that spatial fuel-mix exposure, constructed from public plant registries, complements ESG scores rather than replacing them. Both survive the Fama-MacBeth horse race, suggesting that each captures a distinct dimension of transition risk: ESG measures what firms disclose about their transition plans; spatial fuel similarity measures what the physical network implies about their technology vulnerability.

\subsection{Generalisability Beyond Coal}\label{sec:generalisability}

The fuel-similarity network framework applies in principle to any setting where plant-level technology shocks propagate through shared generation profiles. Gas phase-outs, which several European countries have announced for the 2030s, would enter the model through the same cosine-similarity channel: firms with high gas shares would face stranding contagion when peers retire gas capacity. Nuclear closures present a similar structure, though the smaller number of nuclear operators limits cross-sectional power. Renewable capacity additions are a less natural fit: because the model's mechanism operates through obsolescence signals (a retirement reveals that a technology is uneconomic), positive capacity shocks lack the same informational content. The framework is most informative for transition events that reveal negative information about a shared technology, and least informative for events that are firm-specific or driven by local planning decisions.

%----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

Geographic distance does not insulate power utilities from transition risk. When a firm retires coal capacity, the stock prices of technologically similar peers decline regardless of location. The channel is technology, not geography: fuel-mix similarity captures a common factor that cannot be diversified across locations, while local competitive benefits diversify away in the portfolios of multi-market utilities. Under a shift-share identification strategy with pre-determined fuel-mix shares, the fuel coefficient identifies the causal dose-response of abnormal returns to technology exposure, supported by non-negative Rotemberg weights and an Oster coefficient stability bound of $\delta^* = 20.8$.

Emissions trading amplifies the fuel channel under pooled inference, though the interaction is sensitive to outlier events under Fama-MacBeth estimation. ESG scores and spatial fuel exposure capture complementary dimensions of transition risk, with both surviving a joint horse race. The fuel signal strengthens over calendar time, consistent with gradual market learning about the technology obsolescence channel.

Several caveats apply. The fuel-similarity weight is constructed from installed capacity, not actual generation; a firm with idle coal plants receives the same weight as one actively generating from coal. A subset of firms (105 of 703) use price returns without dividend adjustment, introducing a modest measurement asymmetry. No individual channel survives the Romano-Wolf multiple testing correction across nine hypotheses, though the joint F-test is highly significant ($F = 70.83$, $p = 0.000$) and the fuel channel's Fama-MacBeth $t$-statistic of $-7.36$ exceeds single-hypothesis thresholds by a wide margin.

For practitioners and policymakers, the implication is that transition risk assessment should incorporate the physical network connecting firms through shared generation technology. Spatial fuel-mix exposure, constructed entirely from public plant registries, complements ESG scores and captures a dimension of technology vulnerability that voluntary disclosure does not. Future work might extend the framework to gas phase-outs, where the same cosine-similarity channel should transmit stranding risk to gas-heavy utilities as European decarbonisation targets approach.
