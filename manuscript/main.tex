\begin{quotation}\small\singlespacing\noindent
\textbf{Abstract:} How do financial markets price the credibility of the climate transition? While a growing literature documents systematic climate risk premia and network-propagated financial contagion, existing approaches have not isolated the distinct spatial channels through which physical restructuring shocks transmit to equity prices. Using plant-level geolocation data for 389 listed power utilities worldwide, we show that a single plant retirement transmits shocks through opposing channels: a negative contagion effect for technologically similar firms (stranding risk) and a positive competitive benefit for geographically proximate neighbors. Economically, a one-standard-deviation increase in fuel-similarity exposure translates to a 15-basis-point drop in abnormal returns over three months. Furthermore, we reveal a persistent credibility gap in how markets price these transitions. Idiosyncratic, voluntary plant retirements act as ambiguous signals that spike peer return volatility without moving mean prices, consistent with a cheap-talk equilibrium. In contrast, binding phase-out laws resolve this uncertainty, reducing volatility and generating directional repricing proportional to fossil exposure. Ultimately, our findings show that markets observe physical network exposure but heavily discount voluntary climate action; standardizing corporate disclosure cannot efficiently reallocate capital as long as the underlying environmental policy remains discretionary.
\end{quotation}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

Pricing climate transition risk has become one of the defining challenges in financial economics \citep{Giglio2021}. Meeting the 2$^\circ$C target requires leaving over 80 percent of coal reserves unextracted \citep{McGlade2015}, concentrating stranded asset exposure in specific geographies; under tail scenarios, up to \$24 trillion in global financial assets may be at risk \citep{Dietz2016}. A broad consensus among finance academics and practitioners holds that markets underestimate this exposure \citep{StroebelWurgler2021}, and the difficulty is compounded by deep Knightian uncertainty that propagates across carbon dynamics, temperature dynamics, and damage functions simultaneously \citep{Barnett2020}. The question is no longer whether transition risk is financially material, but whether existing measurement tools can capture its spatial and systemic dimensions.

The financial industry's dominant response has been firm-level scoring: ESG ratings, implied temperature scores, and target validation frameworks that treat transition credibility as a property of the individual firm. This approach suffers from well-documented limitations. Major rating providers disagree on basic assessments of the same company, with pairwise correlations as low as 38 percent \citep{Berg2022}. Even mandatory sustainability reporting fails to produce consistent data across firms, because underlying incentives create strategic variation in what companies disclose and how \citep{Christensen2021}. Corporate climate targets frequently fail basic integrity criteria, and markets exhibit weak reactions when firms miss stated goals \citep{JiangKimLu2025}. Institutional investors overwhelmingly believe climate risks are financially material yet report lacking adequate tools to measure portfolio-level exposure \citep{Krueger2020}. The problem, in short, is not a shortage of data or disclosure mandates but a conceptual gap: firm-level approaches cannot capture risks that originate in the network connecting firms.

A growing body of work suggests that transition risk is fundamentally a network property. Central banks have warned that climate change may trigger ``green swan'' events, systemic crises propagating through cascade effects that conventional risk models cannot capture \citep{Bolton2020BIS}. The post-carbon transition demands systems-level thinking because nonlinear feedbacks in technology, policy, and finance can amplify small interventions into large systemic shifts \citep{Farmer2019}. Network theory has established that systemic risk exhibits phase transitions: the same interconnections that diversify small shocks amplify large ones beyond a critical threshold \citep{Acemoglu2015}. Applied to climate policy, network-based stress tests reveal that direct and indirect exposures to policy-relevant sectors represent nearly half of Euro Area equity portfolios \citep{Battiston2017}, and omitting the financial system from climate transition models creates a dangerous blind spot for scenario analysis \citep{Battiston2021}. These findings point toward measuring transition exposure through the spatial economic networks connecting firms, rather than through subjective ratings of individual companies.

This paper develops and tests such an approach. We construct a spatial network framework for pricing transition risk that bridges the macro-asset pricing literature documenting the scale of the problem, the network finance literature providing the theoretical architecture, and the firm-level evidence exposing the limits of isolated scoring. By measuring exposure through hard physical and economic linkages, specifically fuel-mix similarity, geographic proximity, and regulatory proximity, the framework sidesteps the noise that plagues ESG-based approaches. It also yields a testable distinction: when the direction of a network shock is ambiguous, markets should respond first through volatility rather than directional returns; when binding policy resolves the ambiguity, prices should adjust.

We document three empirical facts. First, spatial transmission exhibits a short-horizon channel split. Over a three-month horizon, fuel-mix similarity predicts negative repricing ($w^{\text{fuel}}_{[-1,+3]}=-1.497$) while geographic proximity generates positive repricing ($w^{\text{geo}}_{[-1,+3]}=0.354$); regulatory proximity is weak. To contextualize these magnitudes, a 0.10 increase in fuel similarity translates to approximately 15 basis points of negative three-month abnormal returns, while geographic proximity yields a modest 3.5 basis-point competitive benefit. At longer horizons (6--12 months), these estimates lose statistical precision, consistent with the gradual incorporation of complex spatial information by informed investors. Second, plant-level retirement announcements (EIA-860 filings) raise neighbouring volatility with no directional return response, consistent with mean-preserving uncertainty. Third, binding phase-out laws reduce volatility and are associated with negative abnormal returns for coal-exposed firms, consistent with uncertainty resolution and stranding. The channel decomposition carries the signal.

To interpret these patterns, we develop a networked asset-pricing framework under policy uncertainty. The framework defines a ``credibility gap'' between physical exposure and what markets can price in real time. Because firms' interests in maintaining fossil operations diverge from investors' climate-risk assessments, voluntary commitments function as a cheap-talk equilibrium \citep{CrawfordSobel1982}, leaving policy uncertainty unresolved. Idiosyncratic shocks (plant retirements) merely inject ambiguous noise, inflating volatility. Only when a binding policy removes managerial discretion does uncertainty collapse, forcing directional asset repricing.

Ultimately, this paper makes three broad contributions to climate finance and policy. First, by documenting the short-horizon channel split, we reveal a paradox in isolated climate action: because geographic proximity provides a competitive benefit ($\gamma_G > 0$), firm-by-firm divestment campaigns risk inadvertently subsidizing neighboring fossil assets. Second, by showing that ambiguous commitments transmit as volatility rather than directional returns, we highlight the structural limits of financial disclosure: standardizing ESG reporting cannot efficiently reallocate capital if the underlying environmental policy remains discretionary. Third, by introducing a plant-level spatial network empirical design, we provide a framework that overcomes ESG-rating noise and bypasses the ``brown-spinning'' illusion, wherein dirty assets are transferred to private markets rather than genuinely retired. The energy transition is a uniquely clean laboratory for these mechanisms, but the implications extend to any networked sector facing systemic regulatory change.

The paper proceeds as follows. Section~\ref{sec:model} develops the empirical framework and lays out the empirical strategy. Section~\ref{sec:results} presents results. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses implications for transition risk assessment and policy. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes. Appendices provide channel definitions, derivations, robustness tables, and data construction details.

\section{Empirical Framework: Networked Pricing under Policy Uncertainty}\label{sec:model}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Overview}

Industry-standard assessment tools --- ESG ratings, implied temperature scores, TCFD-aligned disclosures --- treat each firm in isolation, assuming markets can continuously and accurately price firm-level commitments. We propose a different framework: the \textbf{credibility gap} is fundamentally an information friction. Investors observe the physical network of power utilities and each firm's legacy asset exposure, but they cannot reliably assign a directional price to transition commitments until binding policy removes managerial and political discretion.

We frame transition risk as an asset-pricing problem on a multi-layered spatial network under regulatory uncertainty. This framework yields two core short-horizon insights. First, spatial transmission operates through distinct channels with opposing signs. Second, when the regulatory regime is uncertain, idiosyncratic shocks (e.g., plant-level retirements) act as noisy signals that increase return volatility without a clear directional mean. Conversely, binding phase-out laws partially resolve uncertainty, compress volatility, and generate negative returns proportional to firms' own fossil exposure.

\subsection{Environment and Networked Cash Flows}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

Consider a continuum of listed power utilities $i \in [0,1]$. Firm $i$'s fundamental value is the expected present discounted value of its future cash flows, $V_i = \E[\Pi_i]$. Each firm is characterized by its \textbf{legacy asset intensity}, $\alpha_i \in [0,1]$, which measures reliance on fossil generation (e.g., coal share).

Firms are interconnected via a spatial network decomposed into three row-normalized adjacency matrices: geographic proximity ($\bW^{\text{geo}}$), fuel-mix similarity ($\bW^{\text{fuel}}$), and regulatory proximity ($\bW^{\text{reg}}$). Let $s_j \ge 0$ denote a physical restructuring shock originating at neighbor $j$ (e.g., a capacity retirement). We specify firm $i$'s realized cash flows as a function of own exposure, neighbor shocks, and an unobserved regulatory state $\omega \in \{0,1\}$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cashflows}
    \Pi_i(\omega) = \bar{\Pi}_i - \omega \beta \alpha_i + \gamma_G \sum_j w^{\text{geo}}_{ij} s_j + \gamma_R \sum_j w^{\text{reg}}_{ij} s_j - \omega \gamma_F \sum_j w^{\text{fuel}}_{ij} s_j,
\end{equation}
where $\bar{\Pi}_i$ is baseline profitability. The channels operate as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\gamma_G > 0$ captures \textbf{local competitive benefits}: when a geographically proximate neighbor retires capacity, local grid congestion eases and wholesale margins improve.
    \item $\gamma_R \ge 0$ captures \textbf{regulatory spillovers}: shared jurisdiction effects, which we allow to be positive but noisy at short horizons.
    \item $\gamma_F > 0$ captures \textbf{competitive stranding}: when a fuel-similar peer retires capacity, it signals shared technological obsolescence, but this penalty binds only if the transition regime is enforced ($\omega=1$). The sign is ultimately an empirical question --- technology-similar peers could in principle benefit from shared learning ($\gamma_F < 0$) --- and the data adjudicate.
    \item $\beta > 0$ is the direct penalty on the firm's own legacy assets ($\alpha_i$) under a binding transition ($\omega=1$).
\end{itemize}

\noindent The opposing-sign spatial structure formalizes the distinction between contagion and competitive effects identified by \citet{LangStulz1992}: the same event (peer exit) produces opposite-signed spillovers depending on the dimension of proximity. \citet{JorionZhang2007} confirm this dual-sign pattern in credit default swap markets, where the nature of distress determines whether peer spreads widen (contagion) or tighten (competitive benefit). In electricity markets specifically, \citet{DavisHausman2016} demonstrate that plant closure in a geographically constrained grid increases market power for nearby generators, providing the microeconomic mechanism for $\gamma_G > 0$. The network propagation structure follows \citet{AcemogluCarvalhoOzdaglarTahbazSalehi2012}, who show that idiosyncratic shocks propagate through weighted adjacency matrices with magnitudes determined by network topology; \citet{Herskovic2018} establishes that such production network linkages are priced in equilibrium.

\subsection{The Credibility Gap and Asset Pricing}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

The market does not perfectly observe the true regulatory state $\omega$. Let $p_t = \Pr_t(\omega = 1)$ denote the market's prior probability at time $t$ that the strict transition regime will be enforced.

\begin{definition}[The Credibility Gap]\label{def:credibility}
The credibility gap is the variance in market beliefs regarding policy enforcement, proportional to $p_t(1-p_t)$. When $p_t$ is near $0.5$, uncertainty is maximized, and markets heavily discount stated transition targets because discretionary reversal remains highly probable.
\end{definition}

\noindent Following \citet{PastorVeronesi2012}, who demonstrate that uncertainty about government policy depresses stock prices and generates a risk premium proportional to $p_t(1-p_t)$, we treat this regulatory uncertainty as the core friction driving transition risk. The carbon premium documented by \citet{Bolton2021} is consistent with markets partially pricing this risk ($p_t > 0$) while remaining uncertain about ultimate enforcement ($p_t < 1$). \citet{IlhanSautnerVilkov2021} confirm that option-market pricing of carbon tail risk responds dynamically to such credibility signals.

The market valuation of firm $i$ at time $t$ is the expectation of Equation~\eqref{eq:cashflows}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:valuation}
    V_{i,t} = \bar{\Pi}_i - p_t \beta \alpha_i + \gamma_G \sum_j w^{\text{geo}}_{ij} s_j + \gamma_R \sum_j w^{\text{reg}}_{ij} s_j - p_t \gamma_F \sum_j w^{\text{fuel}}_{ij} s_j.
\end{equation}
The variance of firm $i$'s value, driven by regulatory uncertainty, is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:variance}
    \mathrm{Var}_t(V_{i,t}) = p_t(1-p_t) \left( \beta \alpha_i + \gamma_F \sum_j w^{\text{fuel}}_{ij} s_j \right)^2.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Empirical Predictions}\label{sec:asset_pricing}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

This framework maps different shock types to observable short-horizon returns ($R_i = \Delta \E_t[V_{i,t}]$) and return volatility.

\begin{prediction}[Short-Horizon Channel Split]\label{pred:channel_split}
Holding policy uncertainty $p_t$ constant, a generic vector of physical restructuring shocks $\mathbf{s}$ transmits positively through the geographic network ($\gamma_G > 0$). It transmits negatively through the fuel-similarity network ($-p_t \gamma_F < 0$).
\end{prediction}
\noindent\textit{Derivation:} Immediate from the partial derivatives of Equation~\eqref{eq:valuation} with respect to neighbor shocks.
\textit{Empirical mapping:} This formalizes the channel decomposition design. Rather than a single composite peer effect, spatial transmission splits by sign at the 3-month horizon (documented in Table~\ref{tab:channel_decomp}).

\vspace{1em}

We next evaluate how the market responds to variations in signal clarity.

\begin{prediction}[Ambiguous Shocks: Volatility Transmission]\label{pred:ambiguous}
Suppose an idiosyncratic plant retirement announcement (e.g., EIA-860) acts as a noisy signal of $\omega$. While Bayesian updating may shift $p_t$ marginally, the first-order observable effect for exposed neighbors is a spike in return volatility, with a theoretically ambiguous (or weak) directional mean return due to offsetting geographic and fuel channels.
\end{prediction}
\noindent\textit{Derivation:} With $p_t \in (0,1)$, Equation~\eqref{eq:variance} shows that an increase in $s_j$ strictly increases $\mathrm{Var}_t(V_{i,t})$. Because the signal is noisy, the update to $p_t$ is small, and the opposing $\gamma_G$ and $\gamma_F$ channels mute the directional mean return.
\textit{Literature grounding:} This prediction is consistent with \citet{EpsteinSchneider2008}, who show that ambiguity-averse investors evaluate uncertain-quality signals under a worst-case assessment of precision, inflating perceived variance even when the mean impact is zero. \citet{Veronesi1999} provides the general-equilibrium mechanism: return volatility peaks at maximum posterior uncertainty about the hidden state, precisely where $p_t(1-p_t)$ is maximized.

\begin{prediction}[Resolving Shocks: Directional Repricing]\label{pred:resolving}
Suppose a binding phase-out law is enacted. This systemic shock partially resolves policy uncertainty, causing a discrete jump in beliefs $p_{post} \gg p_{pre}$. For fossil-exposed firms, this strictly reduces return volatility and generates negative abnormal returns proportional to their own legacy intensity ($\alpha_i$).
\end{prediction}
\noindent\textit{Derivation:} As $p_{post}$ approaches 1, the uncertainty term $p(1-p)$ in Equation~\eqref{eq:variance} drops, compressing volatility. From Equation~\eqref{eq:valuation}, the change in value is strictly negative and scales with own coal-share $\alpha_i$.
\textit{Empirical mapping:} Predictions~\ref{pred:ambiguous} and \ref{pred:resolving} distinguish the market reaction to ambiguous filings (volatility increases, no CAR) versus Tier-1 binding laws (volatility falls, negative CAR scales with $\alpha_i$), tested in Table~\ref{tab:volatility_transmission}.
\textit{Literature grounding:} The volatility-collapse prediction draws on \citet{KellyPastorVeronesi2016}, who show across 20 countries that implied volatility drops sharply once political uncertainty resolves. \citet{Bloom2009} documents the canonical pattern: uncertainty episodes approximately double volatility, with rapid normalization upon resolution. The $\alpha_i$-proportional negative repricing is validated by \citet{HsuLiTsou2023}, who show that a pollution premium of 4.42\% per annum is strictly proportional to within-industry emission intensity and driven by environmental policy regime-shift risk.

\begin{remark}[Valuation wedge / credibility gap]\label{rem:credibility_gap}
If firm-level commitments were reliably priced, directional returns would separate credible from non-credible firms around restructuring news. Instead, we observe volatility responses with weak mean returns for ambiguous shocks. We interpret this as a credibility gap: markets observe exposure (fuel mix, geography) but cannot price commitment quality until binding policy resolves the direction. Formally, this friction is captured by the belief variance $p_t(1-p_t)$ in Definition~\ref{def:credibility}. Our model parsimoniously collapses two distinct sources of uncertainty into the single parameter $p_t$: uncertainty about whether \emph{governments} will enforce the transition regime, and uncertainty about whether \emph{firms} will honour voluntary commitments. Both sources leave $p_t$ unresolved because voluntary pledges function as cheap talk in the sense of \citet{CrawfordSobel1982}; only binding regulation acts as a costly signal.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Empirical Strategy}\label{sec:empirical}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

We test the framework on global listed power utilities --- a sector that offers three advantages over alternatives. First, the physical asset base is directly observable: power plants have geolocated coordinates, rated capacities, fuel types, and commission/retirement dates recorded in publicly available registries. This means the legacy intensity parameter $\alpha$ and all spatial exposure measures are built from physical data rather than self-reported disclosures. Second, the spatial network is well-defined: utilities are connected through transmission grids and shared wholesale markets, shared regulatory jurisdictions, and local factor markets for engineering labour and equipment --- connections that are directly documented in public registries. Third, the sector contains large variation in financial fundamentals (from unconstrained Nordic hydro operators to heavily leveraged Southeast Asian coal incumbents) and network density (from isolated island systems to the deeply interconnected European grid), providing the cross-sectional variation needed to test the networked transmission mechanism through return and volatility responses. This single-sector focus follows a distinguished empirical tradition: \citet{Cicala2022} and \citet{FabrizioRoseWolfram2007} demonstrate that electricity generation is a uniquely transparent laboratory where plant-level inputs, outputs, and costs are directly observable, eliminating the measurement noise that plagues cross-sector studies reliant on ESG scores or self-reported disclosures.

\subsubsection*{Sample Construction}

The sample comprises global listed power utilities identified by SIC codes 4911, 4931, and 4932 (electric services) and NAICS 2211 in Compustat Global/NA, cross-referenced with plant ownership records in the Global Energy Monitor (GEM) Global Power Plant Tracker (and GeoAsset where available). We require firms to have (i)~at least one operating power plant identifiable in GEM or GeoAsset, (ii)~financial statements in Compustat Global/NA, and (iii)~equity return data in CRSP (US firms) or Datastream (non-US firms). Appendix~\ref{app:data} documents an interim replication that uses Compustat Security returns when CRSP/Datastream coverage is incomplete. The final sample comprises 389 firms across 50+ countries, with plant-level data covering the bulk of global installed capacity. The event set includes 179 first-mover retirements with exact or announcement dates, 92 EIA-860 retirement announcements, and 27 national or subnational phase-out laws (14 Tier-1 binding shocks). Summary statistics for the analysis sample are reported in Table~\ref{tab:summary_stats}, and regional composition is reported in Appendix~\ref{app:data}.

\subsubsection*{Data Sources and Variable Construction}

\paragraph{Panel A: Firm Fundamentals ($\btheta$).}

The four fundamental parameters $(\lambda,\rho,\kappa,\alpha)$ are constructed from three core databases. Financial statements from Compustat Global/NA provide leverage, an operating-ROA proxy for return spread, and an interest-coverage proxy for cash-flow adequacy. Plant-level asset data from GEM/GeoAsset provide legacy intensity. Equity returns from CRSP (US) and Datastream (non-US) are used for return-based specifications and market adjustment. Full variable definitions and supplementary sources are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:data}.

\paragraph{Panel B: Transformation Metrics (Physical Exposure).}

We compile physical transformation metrics to construct exposure and validate shock classification. These include: (i) capacity mix shifts (changes in clean MW share over 3- and 5-year horizons), (ii) emissions intensity changes, and (iii) binary asset retirement events. Capacity mix shifts are constructed from GEM/GeoAsset plant-level records of additions and retirements; when panel coverage is limited, we use the cross-sectional clean share (1$-\alpha$) to summarise legacy intensity. Emissions intensity uses LSEG ESG Scope~1 data supplemented by implied emissions from capacity mix. These measures are long-horizon outcomes and are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:data}; the main outcomes in this paper are returns and volatility around restructuring shocks.

\paragraph{Panel C: Spatial Weight Matrix.}

The spatial weight matrix $\bW$ is constructed from geographic proximity of plant locations (GEM/GeoAsset GPS coordinates) combined with shared regulatory jurisdiction (ETS membership from the World Bank Carbon Pricing Dashboard; ICAP is used as a cross-check). The geographic component uses inverse-distance weights with exponential decay; the regulatory component is binary (same ETS = 1). The fuel-similarity component $\bW^{\text{fuel}}$ uses the cosine similarity of firm-level generation portfolio vectors (capacity shares across coal, gas, oil, wind, solar, hydro, nuclear), following the text-based network methodology of \citet{HobergPhillips2016}; geographic proximity generates return comovement among co-located firms \citep{PirinskyWang2006}. Following \citet{GrieserHadlockLeSageZekhnini2022}, we also estimate under each component separately. Full construction details are in Appendix~\ref{app:data}.
Sample sizes vary across specifications because return-window availability and exposure coverage drop different event--firm pairs; each table reports its own $N$.

\subsubsection*{Identification Strategy}

The central identification challenge is the reflection problem \citep{Manski1993}: spatial correlation in outcomes may reflect (a)~network interaction effects, (b)~correlated fundamentals, or (c)~common shocks. \citet{BramoulleDjebbariFortin2009} show that peer effects are generically identified when network structure is non-uniform. Our three independently constructed weight matrices provide cross-network exclusion restrictions: firm $A$'s geographic neighbor $B$ need not be fuel-similar to $A$'s fuel-linked neighbor $C$, generating the variation required for identification. We therefore emphasize the event-study strategy tied to the channel-split and uncertainty predictions, and report robustness and ancillary diagnostics in Appendix~\ref{app:robustness}.

A primary identification challenge in spatial asset pricing is the endogeneity of the restructuring shocks themselves. Plant retirements are not randomly assigned; they are endogenous to local market conditions, input prices, and regulatory pressures. Consequently, a naive spatial regression risks conflating true network spillovers with correlated effects \citep{Manski1993} --- for instance, a local macroeconomic downturn could simultaneously trigger a plant retirement and depress the equity returns of neighbouring utilities.

We address this endogeneity through the asymmetric structure of our spatial decomposition. While an omitted common shock would typically induce a uniform directional bias (e.g., depressing all local returns), our empirical design tests for opposing-sign responses across non-nested networks. Specifically, an omitted negative local shock cannot readily explain why geographic proximity yields a \emph{positive} competitive benefit ($\gamma_G > 0$) while fuel-mix similarity yields a \emph{negative} contagion effect ($\gamma_F < 0$). By isolating these channel-differentiated responses, and adjusting returns for market-wide movements, our identification strategy filters out generic macroeconomic shocks to isolate the targeted transmission of transition risk. Furthermore, we leverage the distinct network topology formalized by \citet{BramoulleDjebbariFortin2009}: because firm $A$'s geographic neighbour $B$ is not necessarily its fuel-similar peer $C$, the cross-network exclusion restrictions break the reflection problem inherent in uniform peer groups. We note that a structured confounder --- one that simultaneously benefits nearby firms and penalises fuel-similar ones --- could in principle mimic the opposing-sign pattern. Such confounders are difficult to construct in practice, because they require a single omitted variable to operate with opposite signs along two independent network dimensions; the low pairwise correlation among our weight matrices ($r < 0.16$, Table~\ref{tab:weight_corr}) makes this implausible. Appendix~\ref{app:robustness} validates this strategy empirically, and a firm fixed-effects specification confirms that the channel split survives the absorption of all time-invariant firm heterogeneity (Table~\ref{tab:channel_controls_sensitivity}).

\paragraph{Spatial Transmission in Returns and Volatility.}
We study two types of shocks with different information content.
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Plant-level retirement announcements (EIA-860).} These events convey new information about individual plants but do not resolve the direction of regional adjustment. We test whether exposure affects \emph{volatility} rather than mean returns.
  \item \textbf{Binding phase-out laws.} These events resolve the direction of adjustment at the jurisdiction level. We test whether coal-exposed firms experience negative abnormal returns and lower volatility.
\end{itemize}

\noindent To our knowledge, the use of EIA-860 retirement filings as information events for financial markets is novel; we are not aware of prior studies that have employed regulatory capacity registrations as event dates. The volatility-based approach follows \citet{Beaver1968}, who established that return variance around information events is informative independent of mean price changes, and \citet{HannKimZheng2019}, who demonstrate that corporate announcements transmit volatility to industry peers as a distinct second-moment information channel. For climate policy events, \citet{KochGrosjeanFussEdenhofer2016} validate the event-study methodology applied to discrete regulatory announcements. The coal-share$\times$post-event interaction for binding laws follows the DID specification of \citet{SeltzerStarksZhu2022}, who interact continuous environmental exposure with the Paris Agreement date; \citet{BoltonKacperczyk2023} establish that carbon premia intensify in stricter policy environments, supporting the hypothesis that binding legislation produces measurable repricing.

For returns, we estimate spatial event-study regressions using monthly CARs and channel-specific exposure:
\[
  \text{CAR}_j = \alpha + \beta_{\text{geo}} w^{\text{geo}}_{ij} + \beta_{\text{fuel}} w^{\text{fuel}}_{ij} + \beta_{\text{reg}} w^{\text{reg}}_{ij} + \beta_{s}\text{SameSector}_j + \varepsilon_j.
\]
For volatility, we compute event-time changes in realized volatility and estimate the corresponding exposure coefficients. The primary specification uses vwretd-adjusted returns and event-clustered standard errors; two-way clustering is reported as conservative robustness.

\textbf{Coal-share intensity for phase-out laws.} For binding regulatory shocks we use a continuous treatment intensity: firm coal capacity share (coal MW / total MW) from GEM. This avoids the zero-inflation of spatial weights and aligns treatment with the jurisdictional scope of the law.

\subsubsection*{Robustness, Placebos, and Falsifiability}

The design is falsifiable: if the channel split is absent at short horizons, spatial transmission is not operating through identifiable mechanisms; if retirement announcements do not raise volatility, the uncertainty mechanism is unsupported; if binding laws do not generate negative returns for coal-exposed firms, stranding is not priced. Appendix~\ref{app:robustness} reports window sensitivity and clustering variants.

\section{Results}\label{sec:results}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

The core empirical finding is a short-horizon channel split: the same physical event, a neighbor's capacity retirement, transmits positively through the geographic network and negatively through fuel-mix similarity. We first document this pattern in cross-sectional return regressions, then show that the nature of the shock determines whether markets respond through volatility or through prices. All specifications use market-adjusted returns (vwretd) with event-clustered standard errors; Appendix~\ref{app:robustness} reports robustness across windows, clustering, and alternative exposure definitions.

Table~\ref{tab:summary_stats} summarises the cross-sectional distribution of fundamentals. The most striking feature is the skewness of delivery: most firms in the sample already operate predominantly clean portfolios (median clean share 0.86), while a heavy left tail of fossil-intensive incumbents pulls the mean down to 0.62. This distributional shape matters because the channel-split and volatility results are identified primarily from variation among the fossil-intensive firms that populate that left tail. Leverage is moderate on average ($\bar{\lambda}=0.40$) but varies considerably, from unlevered Nordic hydro operators to Southeast Asian coal generators carrying debt-to-asset ratios above three.

\subsection{Channels and Uncertainty Transmission}

The central question is whether the spatial network carries differentiated information about transition risk, or whether a single composite exposure measure suffices. We address it by regressing firm-level CARs on the three row-normalized spatial weights simultaneously with a same-sector control, as specified in Section~\ref{sec:empirical}, using event-clustered standard errors.

Table~\ref{tab:channel_decomp} reports the channel decomposition. The three-month window reveals a clean split, consistent with the opposing partial derivatives of the valuation equation (Equation~\ref{eq:valuation}). The fuel-similarity coefficient is negative and highly significant ($-1.497$, $t=-3.16$), indicating that firms whose generation portfolios resemble the event firm's experience negative repricing when a neighbor retires capacity, as predicted by $\partial V_i / \partial w^{\text{fuel}} < 0$ (Prediction~\ref{pred:channel_split}). The geographic-proximity coefficient is positive and significant ($0.354$, $t=2.97$), consistent with the local competitive benefit ($\gamma_G > 0$) that arises when a nearby generator exits and wholesale margins improve for the survivors. The regulatory-proximity coefficient is large but imprecise; we retain it to guard against omitted-variable bias and report a two-channel specification in Appendix~\ref{app:robustness} (Table~\ref{tab:channel_decomp_two}), where the qualitative pattern is unchanged. At the six- and twelve-month horizons, the coefficients lose statistical precision under event clustering; the fuel-channel point estimates reverse sign but are not distinguishable from zero, and we regard the longer-horizon estimates as uninformative rather than as evidence of genuine reversal.

To put the magnitudes in context: a 0.10-unit increase in bilateral fuel-similarity exposure corresponds to approximately 15 basis points of differential three-month CARs ($-1.497 \times 0.10$), while the same increase in geographic proximity contributes 3.5 basis points in the opposite direction ($0.354 \times 0.10$). The fuel channel is more than four times as large in absolute value. These are not large effects for individual event-firm pairs, and the decomposition explains a modest share of cross-sectional return variation ($R^2 = 0.0012$). While low in absolute terms, this explanatory power is standard for event-time peer-effect regressions where idiosyncratic noise heavily dominates short-horizon returns (e.g., \citealt{Hou2007}; \citealt{CohenFrazzini2008}). The empirical contribution lies not in the model fit, but in the qualitative channel split: documenting that the same physical event produces opposite-signed repricing along different dimensions of proximity, cumulated across 179 first-mover events.

A natural concern is whether these spatial exposures merely proxy for unobserved firm fundamentals, such as incumbent fossil utilities being structurally larger and more levered. As discussed in Section~\ref{sec:empirical}, our identification relies on the opposing-sign channel split to filter out common shocks; we further address this concern with firm fixed effects. As we detail in Appendix~\ref{app:robustness} (Table~\ref{tab:channel_controls_sensitivity}), introducing cross-sectional controls for size and leverage naturally attenuates the pooled estimates because these variables capture static structural differences between firms. However, when we estimate the model using firm fixed effects to absorb all time-invariant unobserved heterogeneity, the fuel-similarity channel re-emerges with even greater economic magnitude and statistical significance ($-2.023$, $p<0.01$), and geographic proximity remains robust ($0.472$, $p<0.01$). This confirms that our baseline 15-basis-point magnitude is, in fact, a conservative lower bound; the true spatial contagion operates dynamically through within-firm variation around the restructuring event, effectively isolating the network shock from static firm characteristics.

Two features of the figure deserve emphasis. First, the pre-event parallelism is not an artifact of demeaning: raw CARs for high-exposure neighbors are mildly positive before the event, reflecting selection into the high-exposure group (cleaner firms with higher baseline returns) rather than anticipation of the shock. Second, the gradual widening of the gap, rather than an instantaneous jump, is consistent with the information-diffusion interpretation developed in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}: spatial signals are incorporated progressively by segmented investor bases, not absorbed all at once.

We now turn from the first moment to the second. The variance equation (Equation~\ref{eq:variance}) predicts that ambiguous shocks should increase volatility without moving mean returns (Prediction~\ref{pred:ambiguous}), while binding policy shocks should compress the $p_t(1-p_t)$ uncertainty term, compressing volatility and shifting prices in a predictable direction (Prediction~\ref{pred:resolving}). To test these predictions, we construct a measure of realized volatility change: the standard deviation of daily abnormal returns in the twenty trading days following each announcement ($[+1,+20]$), minus the corresponding pre-announcement standard deviation ($[-21,-1]$). We then regress this volatility change on spatial exposure for EIA-860 events and on coal-share intensity for binding phase-out laws, with event-clustered standard errors.

The results, summarised in Table~\ref{tab:volatility_transmission}, confirm the predicted asymmetry. For EIA-860 retirement filings, the volatility coefficient is positive and highly significant ($0.056$, $t=3.89$): firms with greater spatial exposure to the retiring plant experience a measurable increase in return variability in the twenty days following the announcement. The corresponding return coefficient is economically small and statistically indistinguishable from zero ($-0.222$, $t=-0.17$). This combination, higher volatility with no directional price movement, is the signature of mean-preserving uncertainty. The announcement conveys that a neighbor's asset base is changing, which raises the perceived variance of transition-relevant cash flows for nearby firms, but it does not resolve whether the net effect is positive (through the geographic channel) or negative (through fuel similarity).

Binding phase-out laws show the mirror image: coal-share intensity reduces volatility ($-0.130$, $t=-2.53$) and is associated with negative abnormal returns ($t=-1.80$, marginally significant). The volatility decline confirms that binding legislation resolves a component of the regulatory uncertainty captured by $p_t(1-p_t)$ in the framework; the negative returns are suggestive that, once uncertainty is resolved, the market prices the stranding channel.

In sum, the empirical record traces a coherent arc from the framework's predictions to the data. At the three-month horizon, spatial transmission splits cleanly by channel: fuel similarity carries a negative signal, geographic proximity a positive one. When the information event is ambiguous, markets respond through volatility rather than prices. When the event removes discretion, volatility falls and prices adjust in the direction predicted by the stranding channel. Robustness checks on window length, clustering, and alternative exposure definitions are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:robustness}; the qualitative pattern is stable across specifications.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

The core message of this paper is that transition credibility is a network property subject to severe information frictions. Markets observe physical exposure but do not consistently price commitment quality in real time. Instead, transition shocks transmit through distinct spatial channels and through volatility. This section contextualizes these findings within the literature and outlines three specific, counterintuitive implications for investors and policymakers.

\subsection{Information Diffusion and the Limits of Disclosure}

The three-month channel split (fuel similarity $=-1.497$, geographic proximity $=+0.354$) and subsequent loss of statistical precision at 6 and 12 months map directly onto the gradual-information-diffusion literature. \citet{HongStein1999} show that when fundamental information diffuses slowly across investors, prices underreact in the short run and correct as information percolates. Our 3-month spatial effects are consistent with the attention-driven return predictability documented by \citet{CohenFrazzini2008} for economically linked firms, which concentrates at short horizons and then decays. The larger absolute magnitude of the negative fuel channel aligns with \citet{Hou2007}, who finds that negative information diffuses more slowly across linked firms, producing stronger predictability.

More fundamentally, the contrast between volatility spikes following ambiguous retirements and directional repricing following binding laws highlights the structural limits of voluntary disclosure. Under the cheap-talk framework of \citet{CrawfordSobel1982}, because firms' incentives to maintain profitable fossil operations diverge from investors' climate-risk assessments, costless voluntary pledges cannot credibly transmit precise information. \citet{KimLyon2011} document exactly this strategic manipulation in voluntary greenhouse gas registries.

This generates a critical implication for current regulatory efforts. Recent initiatives by financial authorities to standardize ESG disclosures aim to resolve information asymmetry. However, our findings suggest that the primary friction preventing efficient climate risk pricing is not a lack of corporate data, but pervasive political uncertainty. Because markets respond to ambiguous signals with volatility rather than directional repricing \citep{EpsteinSchneider2008}, even perfectly standardized emissions data will fail to shift the cost of capital if the underlying transition policy remains discretionary. Financial regulation (disclosure) cannot substitute for environmental regulation (binding phase-outs) \citep{GreenstoneOyerVissingJorgensen2006, DownarErnstbergerReichelsteinSchwenenZaklan2021}.

\subsection{The Paradox of Isolated Climate Action}

The opposing signs of our spatial channels, mirroring the contagion-versus-competition dichotomy documented by \citet{LangStulz1992}, reveal a paradox in targeted climate activism. While fuel similarity transmits competitive stranding (contagion), geographic proximity provides a competitive local benefit ($\gamma_G > 0$). In electricity markets, the retirement of a local generator reduces grid congestion and increases the market power and margins of proximate surviving plants \citep{DavisHausman2016}.

The counterintuitive implication is that isolated, firm-by-firm climate activism may be self-defeating. If an activist campaign successfully forces a utility to retire a coal plant, the resulting reduction in local energy supply inadvertently increases the profitability of neighboring fossil-fuel plants that were not targeted. Because transition risk is a spatial network property, climate interventions that fail to account for geographic grid spillovers risk subsidizing the very emissions they seek to eliminate. Effective transition policy must operate at the network or jurisdictional level to prevent this spatial leakage.

\subsection{External Validity and the Illusion of Green Portfolios}

The spatial transmission mechanism we document is a specific instance of the general network propagation framework formalized by \citet{AcemogluCarvalhoOzdaglarTahbazSalehi2012}. Any networked infrastructure sector with hub-and-spoke characteristics, such as shipping, pipelines, or heavy industrial supply chains, is predicted to exhibit analogous spatial transmission. \citet{BarrotSauvagnat2016} demonstrate that localized shocks propagate through supply networks, imposing significant sales declines on distant customers. Furthermore, spatial climate risk is demonstrably priced in non-utility asset classes, such as real estate \citep{BernsteinGustafsonLewis2019}.

However, analyzing spatial risk exclusively through public equity markets exposes a critical limitation: the illusion of the ``green'' public portfolio. \citet{DuchinGaoXu2025} document the phenomenon of ``brown-spinning'': publicly traded firms increasingly divest their most pollutive assets to private, non-ESG-rated buyers. Following divestment, the public seller's ESG rating improves, yet the total pollution of the underlying physical asset actually increases.

Because transition risk is tied to the physical asset and its spatial network (as our results show), transferring ownership from a public to a private entity does not reduce systemic risk; it merely hides it from public equity markets. Consequently, climate stress tests that rely solely on the balance sheets of publicly listed firms will systematically underestimate systemic vulnerability. Furthermore, derivative markets \citep{IlhanSautnerVilkov2021} and long-term municipal bond markets \citep{Painter2020} capture tail risks and spatial immobility dynamics that average equity returns may miss.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper establishes that transition credibility is a network property. By mapping the spatial transmission of restructuring shocks across global power utilities, we document a short-horizon channel split: fuel similarity drives negative repricing, while geographic proximity produces a positive competitive benefit. The loss of statistical precision at longer horizons is consistent with gradual information diffusion through spatially segmented investor bases.

Crucially, we show that markets process transition credibility through uncertainty rather than immediate repricing. Idiosyncratic retirement announcements raise volatility without moving mean returns. Only when binding phase-out laws remove political discretion does volatility compress, generating directionally negative returns proportional to a firm's fossil exposure.

Three specific implications emerge for policymakers and investors. First, the limits of disclosure: standardizing ESG reporting cannot overcome the credibility gap if the underlying climate policy remains discretionary. Second, the paradox of isolated action: because plant retirements benefit geographically proximate competitors, firm-by-firm divestment campaigns risk subsidizing neighboring fossil assets. Third, the brown-spinning risk: because transition exposure is embedded in physical spatial networks, transferring dirty assets from public markets to private equity hides systemic risk without reducing it. Ultimately, evaluating transition credibility requires looking beyond the isolated firm to the physical network, and looking beyond corporate pledges to the commitment force of binding law.

Future work should extend the spatial framework to credit spreads, sovereign bond markets, and derivative pricing, where tail-risk dynamics may amplify the channels documented here. Theoretically, while our parsimonious framework captures the first-order pricing of policy uncertainty, extending the model to incorporate strategic interactions, dynamic Bayesian learning, and heterogeneous investor beliefs \citep{BaldaufGarlappiYannelis2020} would further illuminate how spatial signals propagate.

\section{Channel Decomposition and Primitives}\label{app:channels}

This appendix describes how the three exposure matrices in Equation~\eqref{eq:cashflows} are constructed.

\paragraph{Geographic proximity ($w^{\text{geo}}$).} We compute pairwise distances between firms using plant‑level GPS coordinates and define
\[
  w^{\text{geo}}_{ij} = \frac{\exp(-d_{ij}/h)}{\sum_{k\neq i}\exp(-d_{ik}/h)},
\]
where $h$ is the geographic half‑life. We correct standard errors for spatial dependence following \citet{Conley1999}. This captures shared grids, factor markets, and local competition.

\paragraph{Fuel similarity ($w^{\text{fuel}}$).} We build a fuel‑mix vector for each firm from GEM plant records (coal, gas, oil, wind, solar, hydro, nuclear). Fuel similarity is measured as the cosine similarity of these vectors and row‑normalised across neighbours. This captures competitive stranding among firms with similar fossil exposure.

\paragraph{Regulatory proximity ($w^{\text{reg}}$).} We set $w^{\text{reg}}_{ij}=1$ if firms share a carbon‑pricing jurisdiction (ETS membership from the World Bank Carbon Pricing Dashboard; ICAP used as cross‑check), and $0$ otherwise, then row‑normalise. This captures policy spillovers across common regulatory regimes.

These matrices form the empirical channel decomposition used in Section~\ref{sec:empirical}. The composite exposure is not interpreted as a structural parameter; instead, we focus on channel‑specific responses and their horizon‑dependence.


\section{Derivations}\label{app:proofs}

\subsection*{Derivation of Prediction~\ref{pred:channel_split}}
From Equation~\eqref{eq:valuation},
\[
  \frac{\partial V_i}{\partial (\sum_j w^{\text{geo}}_{ij} s_j)} = \gamma_G > 0, \qquad
  \frac{\partial V_i}{\partial (\sum_j w^{\text{fuel}}_{ij} s_j)} = -p_t \gamma_F < 0,
\]
for $p_t \in (0,1)$. The regulatory term enters additively and does not alter the sign of the geographic or fuel channels. \qed

\subsection*{Derivation of Prediction~\ref{pred:ambiguous}}
With $p_t \in (0,1)$, Equation~\eqref{eq:variance} implies
\[
  \frac{\partial \mathrm{Var}_t(V_{i,t})}{\partial s_j} = 2p_t(1-p_t)\left(\beta\alpha_i + \gamma_F \sum_k w^{\text{fuel}}_{ik} s_k\right)\gamma_F w^{\text{fuel}}_{ij} > 0
\]
for exposed firms. Let $y$ denote the announcement signal with likelihoods $L_1=\Pr(y\mid \omega=1)$ and $L_0=\Pr(y\mid \omega=0)$. Bayes' rule implies
\[
  p_t' = \Pr(\omega=1\mid y)=\frac{p_t L_1}{p_t L_1 + (1-p_t)L_0}.
\]
If the signal is noisy ($L_1 \approx L_0$), then $p_t'-p_t$ is small. The mean effect in Equation~\eqref{eq:valuation} remains ambiguous because the positive $\gamma_G$ and negative $\gamma_F$ channels offset, while the variance effect above is strictly positive. \qed

\subsection*{Derivation of Prediction~\ref{pred:resolving}}
Let $p_{post} > p_{pre}$ and suppose the prior is near maximum uncertainty. Then
\[
  p_{post}(1-p_{post}) < p_{pre}(1-p_{pre}),
\]
so volatility in Equation~\eqref{eq:variance} falls. From Equation~\eqref{eq:valuation},
\[
  \Delta V_i = -(p_{post}-p_{pre})\left(\beta\alpha_i + \gamma_F \sum_j w^{\text{fuel}}_{ij} s_j\right) < 0,
\]
which yields negative abnormal returns for coal-exposed firms. \qed

\subsection*{Panel B: Transformation Delivery (Full Detail)}

We also construct physical transformation outcomes for validation and auxiliary analyses. These are not the main dependent variables in the return/volatility tests, but they anchor the exposure measures and provide long‑horizon context. We measure:
\begin{itemize}
  \item[(i)] \textbf{Capacity mix shift} for horizons $k \in \{3, 5\}$ years:
  \begin{equation*}
    \Delta\text{Clean}_{i,t} = \frac{(\text{Renewable} + \text{Nuclear MW})_{i,t}}{\text{Total MW}_{i,t}} - 
    \frac{(\text{Renewable} + \text{Nuclear MW})_{i,t-k}}{\text{Total MW}_{i,t-k}}.
  \end{equation*}
  Source: GEM Global Power Plant Tracker and GeoAsset, which record capacity additions and retirements with dates, fuel 
  types, and ownership. This is our preferred measure because it captures physical transformation that is verifiable 
  and not subject to accounting discretion. In the baseline replication we use the cross-sectional clean share 
  (1$-\alpha$) as a proxy when panel coverage is incomplete.
  \item[(ii)] \textbf{Emissions intensity change}:
  \begin{equation*}
    \Delta\text{EI}_{i,t} = \frac{\text{Emissions}_{i,t}}{\text{Generation}_{i,t}} - 
    \frac{\text{Emissions}_{i,t-k}}{\text{Generation}_{i,t-k}}.
  \end{equation*}
  Source: LSEG ESG (via Workspace) provides Scope~1 emissions for listed firms. For firms not covered by LSEG, we 
  construct implied emissions from capacity mix (GEM) $\times$ technology-specific emission factors (IPCC). Generation 
  data from national regulators and IEA World Energy Statistics.
  \item[(iii)] \textbf{Asset retirement events}: Binary indicator for whether firm $i$ retired fossil capacity exceeding 10\% of its installed base in period $t$. Source: GEM Coal Plant Tracker (retirement year; exact dates obtained from GEM Wiki or news where available) and GEM Gas Plant Tracker.
\end{itemize}

Baseline replication focuses on capacity mix shifts and retirement events; emissions-intensity changes are incorporated when LSEG coverage is sufficient and are reported as a robustness check.

\subsection*{Panel C: Spatial Weight Matrix Construction (Full Detail)}

Baseline replication uses a composite of geographic proximity and shared ETS jurisdiction (the $\bW^{\text{geo}}$ geographic component and $\bW^{\text{reg}}$). Channel decomposition in the main text uses geographic, regulatory, and fuel-similarity components.

$\bW^{\text{reg}}$ \textbf{(Regulatory Proximity)} is constructed from two layers:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item[(i)] \emph{Carbon pricing regime}: $w^{\text{reg}}_{ij} = 1$ if firms $i$ and $j$ operate under the same emissions trading system or carbon tax regime. Source: World Bank Carbon Pricing Dashboard (open access); ICAP Emissions Trading Worldwide Status Report used as a cross-check. This creates discrete clusters: EU ETS, UK ETS, RGGI, China ETS, Korean ETS, and unpriced firms.
  \item[(ii)] \emph{Regulatory framework similarity}: For firms not sharing a carbon pricing regime, a continuous similarity measure based on jurisdiction-level policy indicators in the Climate Change Laws of the World database (Grantham Research Institute/LSE, open access), which codes climate legislation, targets, and policy instruments across 200+ jurisdictions. Pairwise similarity is the cosine distance between jurisdiction policy vectors.
\end{enumerate}
The composite is $w^{\text{reg}}_{ij} = \omega_1 \cdot \text{SharedETS}_{ij} + \omega_2 \cdot \text{PolicySimilarity}_{ij}$, where the $\omega$ coefficients are estimated from the data. Row-normalised.

\medskip

$\bW^{\text{geo}}$ \textbf{(Geographic / Factor Market Overlap)} is constructed from plant-level geolocation:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item[(i)] \emph{Geographic proximity}: Using GPS coordinates from GeoAsset and GEM, compute great-circle distance $d_{ij}$ between each pair of firms' nearest plants. Apply inverse-distance weights with exponential decay:
  \begin{equation*}
    w^{\text{geo}}_{ij} = \exp(-d_{ij}/\bar{d})/d_{ij}, \quad \bar{d} = 500/\ln 2.
  \end{equation*}
  This uses a 500\,km half-life and imposes no hard cutoff. It captures competition for local engineering labour,
  construction capacity, and grid access.
  \item[(ii)] \emph{Technology overlap}: Firms building the same technology (e.g., both commissioning offshore wind in the same maritime region) compete for technology-specific bottleneck resources (installation vessels, specialised turbines, grid interconnection permits). Constructed from GEM project pipeline data using the same geographic kernel:
  \begin{equation*}
    w^{\text{geo},\text{tech}}_{ij} = \frac{\text{shared technology projects within the kernel}}{\text{total projects of firm } i}.
  \end{equation*}
\end{enumerate}
The composite is
  \begin{equation*}
    w^{\text{geo}}_{ij} = (1-\chi) \cdot \text{GeographicProximity}_{ij} + \chi \cdot \text{TechnologyOverlap}_{ij},
  \end{equation*}
  where $\chi \in [0,1]$ is the technology overlap weight, estimated from the data. Row-normalised.

\paragraph{Specification progression.} To ensure transparency in our spatial decomposition, Table~\ref{tab:spec_progression} reports the step-by-step inclusion of our spatial weights. The geographic benefit emerges strongly in isolation and remains highly stable, while the fuel-similarity contagion effect reveals its full magnitude only when estimated jointly with geographic proximity and appropriate firm-level controls. Notably, when estimated in isolation, the fuel-similarity coefficient is attenuated and statistically indistinguishable from zero. This reflects classical omitted variable bias: because geographic proximity and fuel similarity are positively correlated, failing to control for the positive geographic competitive benefit mathematically masks the negative contagion effect of fuel similarity.

\paragraph{Bandwidth sensitivity.} Our baseline geographic weight matrix uses an exponential decay with a 500\,km half-life, calibrated to regional wholesale electricity markets. Table~\ref{tab:bandwidth_sensitivity} demonstrates that our results are robust to alternative network definitions. Shrinking the half-life to 250\,km attenuates the effect as the spatial kernel becomes too restrictive to capture grid-wide wholesale dynamics. Expanding the half-life to 1,000\,km strengthens the geographic coefficient ($0.481^{***}$), consistent with the massive geographic footprint of interconnected transmission systems (e.g., PJM or the European synchronous grid).

\paragraph{Event-time pre-trends.} Event-time analysis of neighbour CARs around first-mover retirements shows that demeaned cumulative abnormal returns are approximately flat in the pre-event window ($\tau \in [-6,-1]$) for both high-exposure and low-exposure neighbour groups, supporting the parallel-trends assumption. We verify the absence of pre-trends following the recommendations of \citet{Roth2022}, who demonstrates that conventional pre-trend tests have low power against relevant violations and can induce distortions in post-treatment estimates; the staggered-adoption estimator of \citet{BorusyakJaravelSpiess2024} is reported as an additional robustness check. Raw (undemeaned) CARs for high-exposure neighbours are mildly positive pre-event, which reflects selection into the high-exposure group (cleaner firms with higher baseline returns) rather than anticipation of the retirement shock.
